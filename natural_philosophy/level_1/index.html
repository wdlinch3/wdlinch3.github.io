Natural Philosophy

Level 1 — Conceptual Orientation

Broad orientation, conceptual framing, and first introductions for readers approaching the project from the outside.

Level 1 is the conceptual entry point for Natural Philosophy. It is meant to be broad, motivating, accessible, and either nontechnical or only lightly technical. Readers should be able to begin here without first committing to the specialized vocabulary of the research pages.

Once upon a time...

... (about 3 centuries ago), Newton wanted to formulate the laws of Nature in a quantitatively precise way. In order to do so, he first had to develop the necessary language. This language became known as "the Calculus", and Newton used it to express his famous laws.

The Calculus of Newton (and Leibniz) is well-suited to the description of particles and forces in Classical Mechanics. However, Newton himself already had philosophical reservations about foundational aspects of it, and anyway this description of Nature is not complete. (To my mind, these two facts are intimately related in a logical way.) Two generalizations of the classical Calculus have been developed that are well-suited to describe Gravitation (Differential Geometry) and non-relativistic Quantum Mechanics (Linear Operator Theory on Hilbert Space).

These extensions are still not sufficient to describe the Quantum Mechanics of light (or even non-relativistic many-body quantum mechanics). For this, the calculus of (relativistic) Quantum Field Theory was developed. Using this calculus, physicists were able to formulate the “Standard Model” of fundamental particle physics which describes all of the known particles and all forces, with the glaring exception of Gravitation.

The Standard Model is wildly successful--unreasonably so even. Fortunately, it is not consistent at very high energies which means that it cannot be more than an approximation to the true workings of Nature. Reasons that I think this is for the best include:

  • It is not mathematically consistent, even in the restricted sense of a theoretical physicist.
  • It is
    • not consistent conceptually as it does not include gravity and thus does not include the most fundamental aspect of spacetime itself.
    • not consistent observationally as it does not
      • allow for neutrino masses or dark matter.
      • not consistent observationally as it does not explain the observed dark energy in the universe.
    • ugly. More precisely, it is a mess of particles in random arrangements.
The Standard Model can be made to be consistent as a Quantum Field Theory resulting in a Grand Unified Theory but it still would not include Gravitation or dark matter. After years of work by many physicists, it has become apparent that a new calculus is needed to describe theories which are fully consistent and include gravity. This calculus is currently under development and the working title for this project is “String Theory”, although more recently it has been realized that "M-theory" is more appropriate.

In its simplest form, Sting Theory is described as a quantum mechanical string moving in ten dimensions. Although this is currently the simplest definition of the new calculus, it is not directly applicable to the description of the Standard Model, which is four-dimensional. The process of reducing to a suitable calculus is called “compactification”. Even if we are not too concerned with the observational and mathematical fact that $10\neq4$
