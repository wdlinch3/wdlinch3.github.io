\section{Linear algebra in 3D}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
In class we learned about vectors as objects that have various properties like addition, a zero, and multiplication by real numbers. 
When we have a dot product, vectors gain additional properties like lengths and relative angles. 
In these supplemental notes we will work through some material on rotations. 
To get interesting results, we will work in 3D. 

\subsection{Vectors and Covectors}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\begin{enumerate}
\item  Let $ 𝒂 =(a¹, a², a³)^T$. The ${}^T$ stands for “transpose” and means that 
$$
𝒂 = \ma{a¹\\ a²\\ a³}
$$
Is a 3-component column vector. Strictly, the qualifier “column” is unnecessary, since--by convention--all vectors are columns. 
Note that the ${}¹, {}²,{}³$ are “upstairs” on the components of $𝒂$. We write these components as $a^i$ with the “index” $i=1,2,3$. 
\item Dual to vectors are “covectors”. These are row-shaped:
$$
α = \ma{α₁, α₂, α₃}
$$
Note that the ${}₁,{}₂,{}₃$ are “downstairs” instead of “upstairs” as on the vector. We write these components of the covector as $α_i$ with the “index” $i=1,2,3$. 
\item The covectors are dual to the vectors in the sense that we can combine the covector with the vector thus:
\eq{\label{MatMult}
α(𝒂) := \ma{α₁, α₂, α₃}\ma{a¹\\ a²\\ a³} = α₁ a¹ +  α₂ a² + α₃a³ 
}
This is an example of “matrix multiplication”. In this case between a $1 × 3$ matrix called a row matrix and a $3 × 1$ matrix called a column matrix.
When using components, we write this as 
$$
α(𝒂) = \sum_{i=1}³  α_i a^i
$$
where $\sum$ (capital sigma) stands for “sum” and instructs you to take the sum of all the terms $α_i a^i$ for $i=1,2,3$. 
When written in this way, we say that the result is given in “components” or “index notation”.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Einstein summation convention} The ∑ symbols are cumbersome and unnecessary, especially when multiple contractions are being carried out:
\begin{enumerate}
\item When writing $∑_{i=1}^n$ we are already making the index explicit, and there is exactly one sum for each pair of repeated indices. 
\item The range is generally understood from context. 
\item When an index is repeated, it is almost always going to be summed. 
\item If indices are being used correctly, summed pairs always appear with one of them “up” and one of them “down”.\footnote{Physicists and mathematicians often disagree about which type is called “covariant” and which type is called “contravariant”. If the group action is defined $V→ V$, it seems natural to call this “covariant”. Then the dual action $V*→ V*$ is called contravariant. }
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Matrices}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
Vectors and covectors are simple examples of matrices that have one column or row, respectively. 
In general a matrix can have $m$ rows and $n$ columns:
$$
M = \ma{M¹{}_{1} & M¹{}_{2} & \dots &M¹{}_{n}\\
	M²{}_{1} &  M²{}_{2} &  \dots &  M²{}_{n}\\
	\vdots &\vdots &\ddots &\vdots\\
	M^m{}_{1} & M^m{}_{2} & \dots & M^m{}_{n}
	}
$$
This is an example of an $m × n$ matrix. The elements of this matrix are written $M^i{}_{j}$ for $i=1,\dots, m$ and $j=1,\dots, n$. 
Sometimes matrices can be multiplied by vectors or covectors or among one another. 
\begin{enumerate}
\item  An $m × n$ matrix $M^i{}_{j}$ can be multiplied by an $n × p$ matrix $N^j{}_{k}$ to form an $m × p$ matrix $(MN)^i{}_{k}$. This is done by multiplying each column of $N$ into the rows of $M$ sequentially according to the rule \eqref{MatMult}:
In index notation 
$$
(MN)^i{}_{k} = \sum_{j=1}³ M^i{}_{j} N^j{}_{k} 
\quad \text{where} \quad i= 1,\dots , m \quad \text{and} \quad k=1, \dots, p
$$
Note that for the sum to make sense, the second index on $M$ must have the same range as the first index on $N$.
After we sum over this index, it disappears and the result does not depend on $n$.
\item A special case is when an $n× n$ matrix $A$ acts on an $n$-component vector $\bf u$: Recall that an $n$-component vector is an $n×1$ matrix. Then 
$$
A {\bf u} = \ma{A¹{}_{1} & A¹{}_{2} & \dots &A¹{}_{n}\\
	A²{}_{1} &  A²{}_{2} &  \dots &  A²{}_{n}\\
	\vdots &\vdots &\ddots &\vdots\\
	A^n{}_{1} & A^n{}_{2} & \dots & A^n{}_{n}
	}
	\ma{u¹ \\ u² \\ \vdots \\ u^n}
	=\ma{A^1{}_{1}u¹ + A^n{}_{2} u²+ \dots +A^n{}_{n} u^n \\ 
	A^2{}_{1} u¹ + A^2{}_{2}u² +  \dots +  A^2{}_{n}u^n \\ \vdots \\ 
	A^n{}_{1}u¹  + A^n{}_{2}u² + \dots + A^n{}_{n}u^n
	}
	=\ma{v¹ \\ v² \\ \vdots \\ v^n}
	= \bf v
$$
%Note that $\bf v$ has components ...
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Synthesis} 
In this section, we studied rotation groups of $𝐑^n$ by looking at some low-dimensional examples. These groups are subgroups\footnote{A subgroup $H⊂G$ of a group $G$ is a subset of $G$ that is also a group.} of a larger groups of linear transformations of $𝐑^n$. The largest is called the general linear group of $𝐑^n$; it is denoted $GL(𝐑^n)$ or $GL(n,𝐑)$.\footnote{In general, if $V$ is a vector space, the group consisting of all linear transformations $V→ V$ is denoted $GL(V)$. The notation $GL(n,𝐅)$ is used for the group of $n× n$ matrices with entries valued in the field $𝐅$. For us, $𝐅=𝐑$ or $𝐂$ (most of the time).} Since group transformations must have inverses, the only condition on such a transformation (besides linearity) is that it be invertible. Equivalently, the determinant of such a transformation is not allowed to vanish. Since this is an “open” condition, the dimension of $GL(𝐑^n)$ is $n²$. 

There were a few main subgroups of $GL(𝐑^n)$ that we considered: 
\begin{itemize}
\item The subgroup $SL(𝐑^n) ⊂ GL(𝐑^n)$ of transformations with determinant 1 is called the “special” group. It has dimension $n² -1$. 
\item The subgroup $O(n) ⊂ GL(𝐑^n)$ of matrices that preserve the Kronecker product is called the “orthogonal” group. From your work on the exponential, you know the dimension of this group should be the same as that of the anti-symmetric $n× n$ matrices, so $\frac {n(n-1)}2$. 
\begin{exercise}
Check this.
\end{exercise}
\item The group $SO(n) = O(n) ⋂ SL(𝐑^n)$ is called the “special orthogonal group”. 
\begin{exercise}
\label{notspecial}
What's an example of an orthogonal transformation that is not special?
\end{exercise}
\begin{exercise}
Check that his group also has dimension $\frac {n(n-1)}2$. 
\end{exercise}
\end{itemize}

The groups $G$ above are all interesting and very nice\footnote{When this term is used in the technical sense, it means that something has a lot of additional structures on it that allow you to do things you cannot assume you may do in general. Good examples are whether you can put a distance measure on it (metric structure) and/or whether you are able to define derivatives (differentiable structure).} spaces called smooth manifolds. (This is in addition to being groups.)  On such spaces, you can do calculus, and in particular, you can define (co)tangent spaces. As a group one point is special, because it corresponds to the identity transformation $𝟏$; its tangent space is denoted $T_𝟏G$ and pronounced “the tangent space to $G$ at the identity”. It is traditional to denote $T_𝟏G$ by the frakture letter(s) corresponding to the lowercase of whatever $G$ is, so $𝔤$ in this case.
From general considerations, this is a real vector space with the same dimension as $G$. The $𝟎∈ 𝔤$ origin of the vector space corresponds to the point $𝟏∈G$. 

For $G =SO(2)$ and $SO(3)$, you computed a basis of $T_𝟏G$ showing that $𝔰𝔬(2) ≃ 𝐑$ and $𝔰𝔬(2) ≃ 𝐑³$. Since we are talking about matrices, the tangent space to the group at the identity is actually an associative algebra, but this is not necessarily the case in general. There is something that generalizes and makes $𝔤$ into a non-associative algebra called the Lie algebra of $G$, and it goes as follows: 
Since we were talking about matrix groups, the commutator is defined. The commutator has an explicit expression in terms of the associative matrix multiplication, but it also has the properties that
\begin{itemize}
\item it is an alternating bilinear map $𝔤 × 𝔤 → 𝔤$. 
\item it satisfies the Jacobi identity: for any $a,b,c∈ 𝔤 $
$$
[[a,b],c] + [[b,c],a] + [[c,a],b] = 0
$$
\end{itemize}
The commutator satisfies the identity precisely because the matrix multiplication is associative (try to prove this), so we can think of this identity as a condition generalizing associativity. 

In general, any vector space $𝔤$ over a field $𝐅$ with a binary operation $[∙ , ∙ ] : 𝔤 × 𝔤 → 𝔤$ satisfying the rules above is called a “Lie (pronounced “Lee”) algebra”. The alternating form is called the Lie bracket. Note that the conditions above do not imply anything about how you can define the Lie bracket explicitly. In particular there does not need to be any way to associate a “product” $ab$ to elements $a,b∈ 𝔤$. 
\begin{exercise}
Show that the cross product $×$ in $𝐑³$ makes $(𝐑³, × )$ into a Lie algebra. Which Lie algebra is it?
\end{exercise}


The exponential map $M ↦ e^M$ was defined on arbitrary matrices, but with a bit more differential geometry, we can define it more generally. In particular, there is an injective map
$$
\exp : 𝔤 → G
$$
called the exponential map that agrees with your calculations in the special case that $𝔤$ is an associative algebra.\footnote{It's quite the pain in the {\it derriere} that we cannot assume $𝔤$ is associative. However, thanks to a \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincaré–Birkhoff–Witt_theorem}{theorem} by Poincaré, Birkhoff, and Witt, any Lie algebra $𝔤$ can be embedded into an associative algebra with identity $U(𝔤)$ in such a way that the Lie bracket in $𝔤$ becomes the commutator in $U(𝔤)$. This algebra is called “\href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_enveloping_algebra}{the universal enveloping algebra} of $𝔤$”.
}
It is important to understand that this map need not be surjective. 
\begin{exercise}
Use your answer to exercise \ref{notspecial} to come up with a counterexample.
\end{exercise}
Since the “linearization map” $G→ 𝔤$ is the local inverse to $\exp: 𝔤 → G$, and since linearization is differentiation, we see that “integration” corresponds to exponentiation. This is a remarkable thing, because in calculus the first derivative of a function $f$ is not usually even close to enough to reconstruct the whole function. 

\begin{exercise}
\label{localiso}
With this observation in mind, suppose you have two Lie algebras $𝔤$ and $𝔥$ that are isomorphic as Lie algebras $𝔤 ≃ 𝔥$. What will be true about their Lie groups $G$ and $H$?
\end{exercise}
