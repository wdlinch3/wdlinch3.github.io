Advanced Astronomy: Solar System

Fall-semester elective · Solar-system astrophysics, geometry, dynamics, and physical reasoning

AA:SS is the part of Advanced Astronomy centered on the Solar System and the astrophysics needed to understand it seriously. It is not mainly a descriptive tour of planets and moons. The point is to understand how celestial motions work, how sizes and distances can be inferred, how the Solar System formed, and how physical reasoning lets us go from observation to explanation.

What kind of course this is

This course is for students who want to understand why astronomical phenomena are true, not merely what they are called. Students read, take notes, work collaboratively, and complete Jupyter Notebook modules using mathematical notation and Python. No prior familiarity with Jupyter, LaTeX, or Python is assumed.

Students who like science and are at least open to quantitative reasoning often find the class rewarding. Students who want astronomy only in a casual, non-mathematical sense may find it less appealing.

Recommended background

There are no formal prerequisites, but students without a solid background in geometry are generally discouraged from starting here. Algebra II is strongly recommended, chemistry is helpful, and precalculus and introductory physics make many topics more accessible. Calculus and AP Physics C: Mechanics are not required, but they deepen what a student can get out of the course.

In practice, the more mathematical and physical tools a student already has, the more depth they can reach. But the course is not reserved only for students who already know everything.

What students study

Celestial sphere

Diurnal motion, rotation and revolution, the ecliptic, seasons, eclipses, celestial coordinate systems, and practical angular reasoning on the sky.

Aristarchus and Eratosthenes

Relative sizes and distances in the Sun–Earth–Moon system, and how geometric reasoning can recover absolute scales.

Kepler and astrodynamics

Kepler’s laws, ellipses, conic sections, conservation ideas, the two-body problem, and the link between Kepler’s picture and Newtonian gravity.

Solar-system structure and formation

Terrestrial and giant planets, moons, belts, comets, the nebular hypothesis, collapse and fragmentation, angular momentum, and the physical origin of large-scale structure.

The Sun

Internal structure, temperature, luminosity, solar wind, basic radiative physics, and fusion as the source of solar energy.

Jupiter

Rotating frames, pseudo-forces, zonal flow, oblateness, Lagrange points, the Trojan asteroids, the phases of hydrogen, and the Jovian moon system.

How AA:X fits in

AA:SS includes Solar-System-specific material, but it also draws on the shared AA:X foundation. Depending on the order in which a student takes the AA courses, some of that shared work may be done inside AA:SS. This includes topics such as geometry, dimensional analysis, astrodynamics, waves and light, and spectroscopy.

Who tends to thrive in AA:SS

Students usually do well here when they are curious about the night sky, willing to derive and calculate, and interested in understanding how astronomical claims are justified. The course is especially good for students who enjoy science, who want stronger applications for mathematics, or who are interested in astronomy competition and later research.
