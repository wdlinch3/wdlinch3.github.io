Advanced Astronomy: Universe

Spring-semester elective · Stars, stellar evolution, galaxies, and cosmology

AA:U is the part of Advanced Astronomy centered on stars, the interstellar medium, stellar birth and death, galaxies, and cosmology. Like AA:SS, it is not mainly a descriptive course. The emphasis is on understanding the physics behind luminosity, spectra, stellar structure, stellar evolution, and the large-scale behavior of the universe.

What kind of course this is

This course is designed for students who want to know not only what astronomers observe, but why those observations imply particular physical conclusions. Students read, take notes, work through Jupyter Notebook modules, and practice combining mathematical, physical, and computational reasoning.

The course is especially appealing to students interested in astrophysics, stellar physics, cosmology, and research-oriented scientific reasoning.

Recommended background

There are no formal prerequisites, but a solid background in geometry is important. Prior completion of AA:SS is very highly recommended. Algebra II and chemistry are recommended, precalculus and introductory physics are helpful, and calculus plus AP Physics C: Electricity & Magnetism make it easier to engage the material in greater depth.

Students who arrive with stronger preparation in logarithms, derivatives, chemistry, and physics tend to have an easier time with spectroscopy, luminosity, stellar structure, and cosmology. Students who begin with less preparation can still succeed, but some topics will be more challenging and may need to be approached more gradually.

What students study

Stars

Parsecs and distance scales, magnitudes and luminosity, blackbody radiation, spectral classification, HR diagrams, and mass–radius–luminosity relations.

Interstellar matter and star birth

Nebulae, atomic and molecular clouds, tracers, the 21-cm line, hydrogen regions, collapse, and the physical conditions that lead to star formation.

Stellar time scales and collapse

Free-fall, Kelvin–Helmholtz, thermal, and nuclear time scales, along with Hayashi tracks, T Tauri objects, hydrostatic equilibrium, and the zero-age main sequence.

Stellar evolution

Giant branches, shell burning, degeneracy pressure, planetary nebulae, white dwarfs, neutron stars, black holes, and the major fusion pathways.

Galaxies and compact objects

Galactic structure, rotation curves, dark matter, supermassive black holes, accretion, and active galactic nuclei.

Cosmology

Hubble expansion, the standard cosmological model, big bang chronology, inflation, nucleosynthesis, and possible large-scale fates of the universe.

How AA:X fits in

AA:U relies heavily on shared AA:X material. Students who take AA:U first will need to complete certain AA:X modules at the beginning of the course and others as needed later on. This does not mean more total work; it means a different ordering and, in some cases, different choices about which Universe modules can be explored most deeply.

Who tends to thrive in AA:U

Students usually do well here when they are interested in using physics and mathematics to understand stars, spectra, evolution, and cosmology. Students who enjoy connecting multiple areas of science, especially physics and chemistry, often find AA:U especially satisfying.

Students who take AA:U after AA:SS usually arrive with a stronger foundation for the shared material. Students who take AA:U first can still succeed, but should expect some AA:X work to be folded in as needed.
