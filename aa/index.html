Astronomy and Astrophysics at TJ

Overview for students, parents, and counselors.

The Astronomy and Astrophysics program at TJ is a multi-layered program that culminates in the Senior Research Laboratory in Astronomy and Astrophysics. Students qualify for that capstone by successfully completing at least one of the two Advanced Astronomy electives: Advanced Astronomy: Solar System (AA:SS) and Advanced Astronomy: Universe (AA:U).

Astronomy is one of the broadest scientific disciplines. It includes observation, imaging, data analysis, simulation, instrument design, and theory. At the same time, it is a genuinely rigorous physical science. Students in these classes are not only asked to learn what happens in astronomy, but also why it happens and how we know.

What the program is trying to do

The Advanced Astronomy courses are designed for students who want to understand astronomical phenomena through physics, mathematics, and scientific reasoning. They are not survey courses in which the main goal is simply to describe interesting objects in the sky. Students read, take notes, work collaboratively, and complete Jupyter Notebook modules that combine scientific writing, LaTeX-style mathematical notation, and Python-based computation.

No prior experience with Jupyter, LaTeX, or Python is assumed. The goal is not to require students to arrive already knowing those tools, but to have them learn to use those tools in a scientific setting.

Program structure

The program has three instructional strands:

AA:SS

The fall-semester course. Focused on the Solar System and the astrophysics needed to understand it in depth.

AA:U

The spring-semester course. Focused on stars, stellar evolution, galaxies, and cosmology.

AA:X

The shared material common to both courses. These modules are completed once, in whichever AA course a student takes first.

This means students may take one course or both, and they may do so in either order. The shared AA:X material is folded into the first AA course a student encounters.

What students actually do

Students encounter new material in a lecture-based setting, take and submit notes, read from the textbook and other assigned sources, and work through collaborative modules. Depending on the topic, module work may be individual or group-based. Assessment includes quizzes, tests, graded notebooks, and other demonstrations of understanding such as presentations and coherent scientific explanation.

Who tends to enjoy these classes

These courses are usually a good fit for students who like science, are at least open to mathematical reasoning, and want to understand how astronomical ideas are derived rather than only memorized. Students interested in astronomy competitions, astrophysics research, or computational scientific work often find the courses especially rewarding.

These classes may be a poor fit for students who want astronomy only in a casual descriptive sense, or who strongly dislike calculation, derivation, and math-based explanation. Students looking for a less quantitative experience may be happier in a club setting rather than in an Advanced Astronomy elective.

Students who are already serious astronomy enthusiasts sometimes do better waiting until they have a little more background in mathematics and physics, since additional preparation can substantially deepen what they get from the course.

How background affects the experience

There are no formal prerequisites, but background matters. Geometry is especially important early on. Algebra II helps with symbolic manipulation and quantitative reasoning. Chemistry is useful for spectroscopy and the physics of matter. Physics helps with dynamics, rotating frames, radiation, and the interpretation of physical models. More advanced preparation in calculus, electricity and magnetism, or quantum mechanics can deepen a student's understanding even further.

In other words: students do not need to know everything in advance, but they will get more out of the courses as their mathematical and scientific toolkit grows.

At a glance

AA: Solar System

Includes topics such as the celestial sphere, seasons and eclipses, Aristarchus and Eratosthenes, Kepler's laws, the structure and formation of the Solar System, the Sun, and Jupiter.

AA: Universe

Includes topics such as stars, magnitudes and luminosity, spectroscopy, HR diagrams, interstellar matter, star birth, stellar evolution, galaxies, and cosmology.

AA:X shared modules

Includes foundational material such as geometry, dimensional analysis, astrodynamics, waves and light, spectroscopy, thermodynamics, relativity, and basic modern physics.
