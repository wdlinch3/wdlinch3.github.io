\subsection{Space, time, and symmetry}

Before we can talk about the laws of motion, we first need to talk about what motion is. In order to do that, we must first address what we can agree about the where and when of events. We'll start with the when.

%%%%%%%%%
\paragraph{Time}
When we talk about events, it is important to establish when they happened. Without thinking much about it, we casually agree on the “now”. No units are needed for this. 

To talk about times that are not now, we have argued for centuries about which standards we should use, eventually settling (at least for the purposes of science) on the unit of seconds.\footnote{This is not as simple as it sounds. Historically, we took 1 second to mean $1/86400$ of a day, but this is way too inaccurate a measure. The modern definition involves a very specific transition in the electron orbitals of a cesium atom.} Once this is done, we can talk about events that will happen “ten seconds from now” or “a minute ago”. 

A device that can measure changes in time is called a clock. Good clocks will “keep time” by always running at the same rate: A good clock does not speed up or slow down in the process of making measurements, but this is possible only if the progression of time is uniform and universal. 
We take this regular progression of time for granted, and it is {\em an assumption} in Newtonian physics that time is that way. 

We also {\em assume} for the purposes of Newtonian physics, that time has always been the way it is now. In particular, there is no “origin of time” before which time did not exist. When we talk about $t=0$, it is understood that we have agreed upon a convention for when to start the clock; there is no implication that times before that do not exist. (Although it can be that the physical situation under study underwent some substantive change when passing from earlier times to $t ≧ 0$. In this sense, there is usually also some final time after which something changes.)

In summary: Time $t$ is not absolute, but changes in time $Δt$ are measurable. A second system of time $t'$ is considered equivalent to the first if they differ only in origin ({\it i.e.}\ what is being called $0$ in either system). In other words, they will be equivalent if there is some real constant $c∈ 𝐑$ such that $t'  = t+ c$. When this happens, two events $f$ and $i$ will be separated in time by the same amount:
$$
Δt' = t'_f - t'_i = (t_f +c) - (t_i +c) = t_f-t_i =Δt 
$$

On a more mathematical note: We can identify the collection $𝐓 = ❴t❵$ of all valid times with the real numbers $𝐑$, but to do so, we have to pick which time will be identified with the number $0$ and which with the number $1$. After that, regularity in time will line up with the analogous regularity in the real number system. Picking these two things “selects an origin” and “sets the scale” we call seconds. Mathematically, we are saying that after you have picked these two pieces of information, you may identify $𝐓 = 𝐑$. \footnote{Mathematicians do not like to use the symbol $=$ for things that equal in a way that is different from how two numbers are equal. In this case they would write $𝐓 ≅ 𝐑$ say that “the space of all times is {\em isomorphic} to the space of real numbers, but not canonically so. The use of “not canonically” means that there were some choices involved.}



%%%%%%%%%
\paragraph{Space}

As humans we take for granted that we can move in 3 independent directions we call “dimensions”: forward/backward, left/right, and up/down. Each of these directions is just like the others (except that we find it much harder to move in the third one because of gravity, but we'll get to that). 

Many of the physical phenomena we will study take place in only 2 or even 1 of the 3 dimensions available. For example, a ball falling from rest under the influence of gravity moves only in the vertical direction. 
Because of this and the previous remark, we focus first on the case where all of space is just one dimension. 

One-dimensional (1D) space is very similar to time: 
Similarly to “now”, we have “here”, and similarly to “1 second from now” we have “1 meter from here”: Again, we had to decide on a convention for how we will measure distance. Analogously to the clock, there is a device to measure distance called a “ruler”. 

Also, just as with time, there is no “origin of space” and any point is in this space is just like any other. This again makes it different from $𝐑$ which has a special point called $0∈ 𝐑$. 
Just as with time, we can pick a point in space to identify with this special $0$. We can then pick a point a distance of 1 meter from the first and send that to $1∈𝐑$. This fixes where all the other points go, and we again have that 1D space can be identified with $𝐑$, but notice that this is an independent identification from the one we made for $𝐓$. (It may help to think of each as having its own copy of $𝐑$ for this.)

Finally, note that--just as with time--two coordinate systems for 1D space will be equivalent if they differ only by a translation. In formul\ae{} if we denote a valid coordinate system by $x$ and another by $x'$ then there is a constant $a∈𝐑$, so that $x' = x+a$. This again, makes it so that distances will agree in both coordinate systems.

Now let's add a dimension: Instead of just forward/backward, we can now also move left/right. The new direction is just like the first and--importantly--they now share a unit: The ruler we defined before will also be used for this additional direction. The two directions are essentially the same {\em except} that they extend in different “directions”. In fact, in 2D, we cannot only measure distance, but also angles (with a protractor). 

A {\em Cartesian coordinate system} for this 2D space consists of the following information:
\begin{itemize}
\item a choice of origin
\item a choice of “$x$-axis” taken to be a direction in 2D to be identified with $𝐑$ as above
\item a choice of “$y$-axis” required to lie along the unique direction orthogonal to the first and an identification of it with another copy of $𝐑$.
\end{itemize}
It is always possible to choose a Cartesian coordinate system for 2D space so, again, up to the choices made, we see that 2D space can be identified with the coordinate plane $𝐑²$ from coordinate geometry and algebra. 

However, this time something is new: Whereas previously two coordinate systems were equivalent only if they differed by a translation, now they can differ by a 2D translation and/or a {\em rotation}. This is so because translations and rotations both preserve distances and angles. 

Adding a third direction proceeds much as before, but there is a complication we glossed over previously: When we added the second direction in the cartesian coordinate system, we didn't say which way it should “point” ({\it i.e.}\ which direction orthogonal to the first gets to map to $+1 ∈ 𝐑$). In 3D, this is irrelevant because we can rotate around the $x$-axis to turn the $y$-axis up-side down, so to speak. 

However, to attach a third axis, we now really do need to decide on yet another convention as to which way it should point. 
We do so with the following rule: hold your right hand open with out-stretched fingers pointing along the $x$-axis. Then curl your fingers toward the positive $y$-direction. While doing this, stick out your thumb: That is the positive $z$-direction. 

It sounds like a joke, but sadly it is not. This is the so-called right-hand rule for completing a Cartesian coordinate system in 3D by assigning the positive $z$-direction. Such a coordinate system is called a “right-handed coordinate system”. 

\begin{exercise}
Define a left-hand rule, and use it to construct a left-handed Cartesian coordinate system. 
Can you rotate your new system of coordinates into a right-handed system?
\end{exercise} 

\paragraph{Our Galilean Space-Time}
A change of Cartesian coordinate system is called a coordinate transformation. In the examples above we had primed and unprimed coordinates $(t, x, y, z)$ and $(t', x', y', z')$, and we can imagine transforming one into the other. 
We have already enumerated these:
\begin{description}
\item[Time translation] $t→ t' = t+c$ for some $c∈ 𝐑$. In this case, we keep everything else fixed but just shift all times by a fixed amount. (Kinda like daylight savings time...)
\item[Space translation] $x^i → (x')^i=x^i + a^i $ for $i=1,2,3$ for the three directions and some fixed triplet of numbers $a^i∈ 𝐑$ for each $i$.
\item[Rotation] In addition, we can rotate the unprimed coordinate system to get a new one. (We won't write the formul\ae{} for this, since it would take us too far outside of the scope of the class.) If we start with a right-handed Cartesian coordinate system, we will get another right-handed Cartesian coordinate system. 
\end{description}
The number of parameters we need to specify such a transformation is 7: 3 rotation angles + 3 translations + 1 time translation.

Space and time do not change when we transform coordinates in this way. 
Because of this, these transformations are often referred to as “symmetries” of space and time, or “spacetime symmetries”.

\subsection{Particles} We will be talking a lot about particles. By this we will mean a point-like particle, or a particle taking up no volume in space. For the purposes of mechanics, this is an idealized notion, since all physical objects with which we are concerned in classical physics take up some volume in space. 

Nevertheless, there are excellent reasons to use this idealized concept. The first is that a physical object can be described very accurately as a collection of point-like objects. If you like, you may think of them as being the positions of the physical atoms out of which they are made up. 

A second important reason to talk about point-like particles is that the laws of nature that physical objects obey can often be shown to be equivalent to simpler laws about point particles that inherit certain properties of the original object. For example, the motion of a brick of mass $m$ as it slides off of a roof is equivalent to the motion of a point particle of mass $m$ located at a certain point within the brick called the brick's “center of mass”. (We return to this concept in section \ref{S:CenterOfMass}.)

The position of a point particle is an absolute thing, but to describe it, we need to refer it to something else: To describe where to find your phone, someone must refer to it relative to something you already know how to find. One method to do this is to set up a system of coordinates in which we agree on an origin, and three mutually-independent directions which we usually take to be orthogonal. Even more arbitrarily, we conventionally refer to the distances along these axes by the variable names $x$, $y$, and $z$. The point with coordinates $x=0$, $y=0$, and $z=0$ is called the origin $O$ of the coordinate system. Once we agree on such a system, the position of a point particle can be described by providing exactly 3 real numbers called the coordinates of the particle with respect to this coordinate system.

The fact that it takes exactly 3 coordinates to describe the position of something is what makes that thing a particle in 3 dimensions. If we were to describe a string, we would need (infinitely) many more points since every point on the string is a point particle. If we were to describe a sheet, we would need even more than that, and so on.

\subsection{Newton I: Inertial frames of reference}
We want to be able to describe the laws of nature {\it quantitatively}\index{quantitative}. 
This means that we want to not only say what phenomena will take place ({\it i.e.}\ qualitatively)\index{qualitative}, but also how much, how far, how fast in numerical terms.
The field of mathematics came into existence precisely to formulate and answer such questions, so we need to establish the correct mathematical framework. 

Newton's First Law does precisely this. It asserts the existence of a system of coordinates that is in a state of absolute rest and that this system is the familiar one from geometry.\footnote{Mathematically, we say that this is 3-dimensional Euclidean space.} 
Time is similarly universal, so everyone agrees as to what events are happening simultaneously. 
This also allows us to compare states of motion in quantitative terms since we can measure angles, distances, and times. 
The system just described is called an {\em inertial frame of reference}\index{reference frame, inertial frame (of reference)}, {\em inertial reference frame} (IRF). 

Galileo realized that the rules of nature 
%were the same in a moving frame of reference 
in this inertial frame of reference were not special to this very special frame. 
The rules would be the same in a frame of reference moving relative to the one that is in a state of absolute rest {\em as long as that frame was moving at a constant velocity relative to it}.\footnote{The usual example is to think of a train that is moving at constant speed in a straight line. As long as you don't look outside, you might not notice that you are moving relative to the ground. Of course you can always tell in a real train because of the noises, vibrations, and the fact that the track is not perfectly straight and level. The situation was far worse for Galileo who reportedly tried doing some of his experiments on horseback!}
Because of this, we generalize the definition of inertial reference frame to include any and all frames of reference that are moving at constant velocities (sizes {\em and} directions!) relative to one absolutely at rest. 
It follows from this that any frame of reference moving at a constant velocity relative to an inertial one is itself inertial.\footnote{This is an example of what mathematicians would call an \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation}{equivalence relation}.}

\paragraph{Newton I}
Inertial frames of reference are realized in nature, and in such an inertial frame of reference, objects that are free of any interference will continue in their state of motion.
This state of motion is to either be at rest or to be moving in a straight line at a constant speed. 
%\end{result*}

Newton also formulated the notions of mass as a quantity of matter and (linear) momentum as a quantity of motion. 
With these, we can restate the first law as 

\paragraph{Newton I (v2)}
Inertial frames of reference are realized in nature, and in such an inertial frame of reference, the linear momentum of an object does not change in time. 

In modern language, we would say that in an isolated system in an inertial reference frame, the linear momentum is conserved.

\subsection{Newton II: Forces and motion}

Newton's second law is about what forces are and how they act to change an object's state of motion. 
For us a force will be a push or a pull in some direction with some strength. Multiple forces $𝐅₁, 𝐅₂ , ...$ can act on a single object, but that single object can only have one state of motion/momentum $\bf p$. 
Newton's second law says that the relevant force affecting the object is the vector sum of all the individual forces. This is called the net force:
$$
𝐅_{net} = 𝐅₁ + 𝐅₂ + ... + 𝐅_N = ∑_{i=1}^N 𝐅_i 
$$

\paragraph{Newton II}
In an inertial frame of reference, the rate of change of an object's linear momentum $\bf p$ is given by the net force acting on that object:
$$
𝐅_{net} = \frac{Δ \bf p}{Δ t}
$$

Note that this means that any violation of the conservation of momentum implies that the system is not isolated and a net force is acting on the object. Conversely, in an IRF, a net force on an object will result in a change of the momentum. 

A very important special case is when the mass $m$ of an object is not changing. (This is often what we are thinking of anyway, but it does not always have to be the case.) In this case, the quantity of motion simplifies to 
$$
{\bf p} = m {\bf v}
$$
where $m$ is the (now unambiguous) mass and $\bf v = \frac{Δ \bf x}{Δt}$ is the velocity. In this situation, Newton's second law simplifies to a more familiar form:
\paragraph{Newton II (v2)}
In an IRF for an object of mass $m$, a net force accelerates an object according to 
$$
𝐅_{net} = m {\bf a} 
$$
{\em Proof:} In this case
$
Δ  {\bf p} = {\bf p}_f -{\bf p}_i =m {\bf v}_f - m{\bf v}_i = m({\bf v}_f - {\bf v}_i) = m Δ {\bf v}
$,
so $\frac{Δ \bf p}{Δ t} = m \frac{Δ \bf v}{Δ t}$. Since the acceleration ${\bf a} = \frac{Δ \bf v}{Δ t}$ is just the rate of change of the velocity, the result follows from the more fundamental formulation of Newton's second law. 

The mass in this formulation acts as a resistance to a change in motion: If we fix the force, but double the mass, the size of the resulting acceleration will be less by a factor of $\frac12$. If we triple it, the acceleration is less by a factor of $\frac13$, and so on. Very massive objects accelerate very slowly whereas it takes very little force to get a tiny mass to change its velocity. For this reason the mass appearing on the right-hand side (RHS) of the equation of Newton's second law is sometimes called the {\em inertial mass}\index{mass; inertial}.

Next, we will consider some important examples of the forces appearing on the left-hand side (LHS).

\subsubsection{Weight}
In the section on kinematics, we have been using that all objects fall at an acceleration of $g ≈ 9.8\ m/s²$ vertically downward. Intuitively, we know that this is happening because of a force pulling the book downward that we refer to as gravity\index{Force; gravity}. But what is the size of this force of gravity?
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[height=20mm]{/Users/wdlinch3/Dropbox/NoVa/TJHSST/Teaching/Physics/ClassNotes/Figures/Forces/Book1.png}
\end{center}

When a book with mass $m$ rests on a table, it is not accelerating downward since the force of the table on the book $𝐅_{tb}$ is keeping it from doing so. By Newton II, a book at rest is experiencing no net force, so the force of the table $𝐅_{tb}$ must be equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the force of gravity $𝐅_{g}$:
$$
F_{tb} - F_g = m a_y = 0
$$ 
(There are no horizontal forces at all, so we ignore that component of Newton II.)
If we somehow suddenly remove the supporting table, the only thing that changes is that there is no $F_{tb}$ term:
$$
- F_g = m a_y 
$$ 
Since the acceleration is known to be $a_y = - g$, we find that the force of gravity is
$$
F_g = m g
$$ 
This combination is called the {\em weight}\index{weight} of the book. Notice that the weight is {\em not} a quantity of matter (mass), but a force. Specifically, it is the size of the gravitational force experienced on the surface of the earth by a mass. 

Notice that while we were finding this result, we used that the magnitude of the acceleration of a freely falling object was $a = g$. Then the LHS of the equation in Newton II is $F_g=mg$ while the RHS is $ma$. So when the only force acting on a mass is gravity, Newton II reduces to 
$$
mg = ma\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad g = a
$$
and the mass cancels out.\footnote{If something feels like a cheat to you right now, you might be a talented physicist! The forces appearing in this section are on the “left-hand side” of Newton II so the mass appearing here “belongs” with those LHS forces. When it comes to the force of gravity, you might call it the “gravitational mass” in the sense that it is related to how strongly the force of gravity acts on it. 

In contrast to this, what appeared previously ({\it i.e.}\ on the RHS) is the resistance to a change in the state of motion, that is the “inertial mass”. 
Considered in this light, this cancelation greatly bothered Newton who could not figure out any justification for the “inertial” mass having to be {\em exactly the same thing as} the “gravitational mass”; in principle they could have been two different things. Einstein's theory of gravitation can be said to fix this by making it so that the two concepts cannot be distinguished.

\subsubsection{Normal force}
The force of the table on the book above is an example of a {\em normal force}\index{normal force}. These forces are called that because they are pointing in a direction orthogonal to the plane of contact, and another word for an orthogonal angle is “normal”. 

\rightfig{RampCart}{40}{Frictionless cart on an incline}

Later, we will consider books and the like on inclined planes. In these cases, the force of the plane on the book is again normal to the plane. If the plane makes an angle $θ$ with the horizontal, the normal will make the same angle with the vertical.

%\begin{example}
\paragraph{Cart on a ramp}

\rightfig{RampCartFBD}{50}{In a free-body diagram for an incline, it is a good idea to decompose in directions along the incline (parallel or $\parallel$) and perpendicular to it (perpendicular or $⊥$).}

Consider a frictionless cart on a table that is inclined with respect to the horizontal by an angle $θ$. Let's calculate the acceleration of this cart. 

The first thing to do is to draw our free-body diagram (FBD). It consists of one dot (the cart) and two vectors emanating from it: the force of gravity $𝐅_g$ pointed straight down and the normal force $𝐅_N$ pointing upward at an angle $θ$ from the vertical and orthogonal to the direction along the inclined plane. 

Next we want to apply Newton II to this dot-with-arrows. Since there are only two vectors and they are not pointing in opposite directions, the net force $𝐅_{net}= 𝐅_g + 𝐅_N$ cannot be 0, so the cart must accelerate. Of course, it will accelerate down the ramp. In particular, it will not accelerate in the normal direction. This means that the normal force must be completely balanced by part of the gravitational force. 

To do calculations, we must decompose the forces and solve the components of Newton II. Naïvely, we would decompose in the vertical direction and the horizontal one, but this is not the most clever thing to do. In such components, the acceleration will have both vertical and horizontal parts and to find the size of the acceleration in the actual direction of motion, we'd have to use Pythagoras and make a mess. 


A much better thing to do is to “rotate our axes”: Instead of to the right and upward, we pick “down the ramp” and “orthogonal to the ramp (upward)”. When this happens, the acceleration $\bf a$ is in the former direction and $𝐅_N$ is pointing along the latter. We do still have the problem that $𝐅_g$ is pointing along neither of these, but now we can just decompose: You should verify that the part along the ramp $F_\parallel = mg \sin θ$ and that the part orthogonal to the ramp $F_⊥ = - mg \cos θ$. (Note the signs.) The component Newton II equations are now 
\al{
F_N + F_⊥ = m a_⊥ = 0 
	\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad 
	F_N - mg \cos θ =0
\cr
F_\parallel = m a_\parallel  
	\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad 
	mg \sin θ = m a_\parallel 
}
Notice that the first equation gives us the magnitude of the normal force, and the second gives the acceleration of the cart.
Since the only acceleration is $a_\parallel $ we simplify the equations and call this $a$:
$$
F_N  = mg \cos θ
	\quad \quad \mathrm{and} \quad \quad 
a = g \sin θ
$$

We have derived the equations we sought, but we are not done: The next step is to listen to what they are telling us. 
The first equation gives the normal force as the weight but adjusted by a trig factor $≤ 1$. When $θ → 0$, we get the full weight as before. When $θ → 90^∘$, $F_N→ 0$. This makes sense because there is no force keeping a cart on a vertical surface. 
We also notice that when $θ→0$, there is no acceleration, which is as it should be for a card on a horizontal surface, and when $θ → 90^∘$, $a→ g$ which exactly what we expect for a cart in free-fall. 

Finally, for the second equation, we see that the mass has canceled out of the equation for the acceleration. We might think something is wrong with this, but a little reflection and dimensional analysis suffices to convince us that we cannot construct any equation involving the mass. (There are no other variables in the problem with the dimensions of mass, and mass does not appear in the dimensions of acceleration.) {\em This cancelation from the acceleration is generic.} Do not get confused if an acceleration problem does not give you a mass; it probably means it will cancel out in the end. If you like, you can introduce a mass, use it in calculations, and watch it cancel out in the end. 
%\end{example}

\subsubsection{(Non-)Contact forces}

Normal forces are examples of a type of force called {\em contact forces}\index{force; contact}, because contact is required for the force to exist: Lift the book off the table by picking it up with two hands and there is suddenly no normal force defined. Of course, there are now the forces of your hands acting on the book which are again contact forces. 

An example of a non-contact force is the force of gravity. An apple in free-fall on the moon is not in contact with anything.

\subsubsection{Friction}\label{S:Friction}
Friction is one of the most important forces there is, since there is very little we would be able to do without it. When we walk, we push backward on the ground, and friction with the ground is what pushes us forward. When we slip, we do so for want of friction, and we often fall down. When we fall down, we stop sliding around because of friction, {\it et cetera}. 

\rightfig{RampBook}{40}{Book of mass $m$ on an incline with angle $θ$}

Consider again the cart on the inclined plane, but replace it with a book. If the incline is not much, our general experience is that the book will not slide. This is because the force of friction is balancing the parallel component of the gravitational force. We can use this to find the size of the force of friction: As long as the book does not accelerate, the parallel part of Newton II tells us that 
$$
- F_f + mg \sin θ = ma_\parallel = 0
\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad 
F_f  = mg \sin θ
$$
Note that when $θ→ 0$, $F_f → 0$. This makes sense because if there were still some non-zero $F_f$, it would be unbalanced and the book would accelerate from rest. (Think about that: You put the inclined plane with a non-moving book back to horizontal and suddenly the book accelerates to the left?!) Apparently, we need to apply an external force in the direction parallel to the plane in order to have a force of friction. The force of friction will then act to oppose the applied force. 

As we increase $θ$, the applied force increases, and---for a while---so does the force of friction thereby keeping the book from accelerating. Notice that the force of friction is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction while this is happening. 
Eventually at some angle, the force of friction cannot increase any more beyond some maximum $F_{f,max}$ and the book suddenly accelerates down the ramp. 
There is still a force of friction, but it is no longer as large as the gravitational component along the ramp: $F_f < mg \sin θ$. This component of Newton II now reads 
$$
-F_f + mg \sin θ = ma 
\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad 
a = g \sin θ - \frac {F_f}m > 0 
$$

Although friction is still acting, the abruptness with which the change of motion happens suggests that the force of friction for the sliding book is less than the maximum force of friction $F_{f, max}$ for the book at rest. In fact, the acceleration is usually substantial even when the angles just before sliding and just after are almost equal. This can only happen if the force of friction during sliding, is significantly less than the maximum when it is not. 

We refine the relevant lingo by referring to the force of friction for a static book as, well, {\em static friction}\index{Force; friction; static}. We usually label $F_f$ in this case as $F_{f,s}$ or just $F_s$. The maximum force of static friction $F_{f,max}$ is similarly re-labled to $F_{fs,max}$ or $F_{s,max}$. The force of friction while the object is moving is referred to as {\em kinetic friction}\index{Force; friction; kinetic} and labeled as $F_k$. The following inequalities hold for the various forces
$$
F_s ≤ F_{s,max}
\quad \quad \textrm{and} \quad \quad 
F_k < F_{s,max}
$$

\paragraph{Coefficients of friction}
We still have not said anything about how to determine these forces, but we have had so much experience with them, that we might be able to figure it out. 

For example, if you are trying to keep something from sliding, one way to do that is to put something heavy on it. In doing so, you are also increasing the normal force. As the excellent physics student that you are, you could design an experiment to find that there is a linear relationship between the maximum force of static friction and the normal force: They are proportional in size. (They cannot be proportional as vectors, because they point in orthogonal directions.) The coefficient of proportionality is called $μ$ (the Greek lowercase letter mu), or because this is for static friction $μ_s$:
$$
F_{s,max} = μ_s F_N
$$
Since $F_{s,max}$ and $F_N$ are both forces, $μ$ must be dimensionless ({\it i.e.}\ have no units). The size of the {\em coefficient of static friction}\index{Coefficient of friction; static} depends on the type of interface in question. For things sliding on lab tables, a typical value is something like $μ_s ~ 0.5$. 
\begin{exercise}
What would it mean for $μ_s >1$?
\end{exercise}

Encouraged by your success, you might then try to determine the coefficient of kinetic friction using the inclined plane. In this case you might need to figure out a way to determine the magnitude of the acceleration as you vary the mass. If you are successful, you can then use the equations derived above to calculate the force of kinetic friction and you would again find it to be proportional to the normal force: 
$$
F_k = μ_k F_N
$$
The proportionality constant is now called the {\em coefficient of kinetic friction}\index{Coefficient of friction; kinetic}. A typical value in this case might be $μ_k ~ 0.2$ or $0.3$. 

The rules for friction are this: 
\begin{itemize}
\item For a static object, the force of friction is that of static friction $F_s$. Its magnitude is equal to that of the vector sum of all the forces, but its direction is opposite to it. If the size of the others change, it changes to match so that the net force stays zero. This continues until a limit in the maximum size $F_{s,max}$ is reached. This maximum size is proportional to the normal force on the object as described in the equation above. Note that the size of the force of static friction is always smaller than the max but there is no minimum since it depends on how small/large the remaining forces are: $0 ≤ F_s ≤  F_{s,max}$.
\item For an object in motion (accelerating or otherwise) the force of friction is kinetic. It is again opposite in direction to the sum of the remaining forces, but it always has the same magnitude. Its relation to the normal force is as given in the equation above. 
\item The constants of proportionality $μ_s$ and $μ_k$ are properties of the materials involved, but they always satisfy $μ_k < μ_s$, because the force of kinetic friction is strictly less than the \underline{maximum} force of static friction. 
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Tension, strings, and pulleys}\label{S:Tension}
The force of tension is the name for the pulling force along a string. If we are interested in the magnitude, we may refer to the tension to mean the magnitude of the force of tension. However, be warned that care is not always exercised when it comes to treating forces and their magnitudes.

\paragraph{Ideal string}
A string is considered ideal if it is massless, bends effortlessly and without friction, and does not stretch. Since it is massless, it will not participate in the dynamics of the underlying problem and there is no FBD for it. Since it does not stretch, the distance between two objects connected by a string does not change. Consequently, the magnitude of the acceleration of one object is equal to that of the other to which it is attached. 

\rightfig{IdealPulley}{40}{An ideal pulley has equal tensions on the two sides}
\paragraph{Ideal pulley}
Strings cannot push but they are very useful for redirecting a force. 
The device that redirects a string is called a {\em pulley}\index{pulley}. Since strings can redirect force, pulleys are used to redirect forces. A pulley that can do this without altering the dynamics of the problem is called an {\em ideal pulley}\index{pulley; ideal}. 
Ideal pulleys have no mass and no frictional forces, so again, they do not appear in an FBD. 
%It follows from this that the tensions on a string on opposite sides of a pulley are equal. 
What this really means in the end is this: {\em For an ideal pulley, the tension on one side is the same as on the other side.}

\rightfig{PulleyPiano}{80}{A pulley system called a “Luff tackle” to raise a heavy object}

\paragraph{Engineering application}
Pulleys are very useful as “force multipliers”. Consider the pulley system in figure \ref{F:PulleyPiano}. The mass of the larger grand pianos is about $M = 600\ kg$, corresponding to a weight of $6\ kN$ (~1300 lbs). In this pulley system, there are three cables holding it up, all at tension $T$, if the pulleys are ideal. If the system is in equilibrium, then the net force on the piano must vanish. For the vertical components, this means that $∑ F_{vert} = Mg - 3T = 0$, or $T = \frac{Mg}3 = 2\ kN$ (~450 lbs).

The pulley arrangement used here is called a “\href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulley}{Luff tackle}”. It has “ideal mechanical advantage of 3”.
There are pulley “block and tackle” assemblies with many more cables to further raise the advantage and lower the tension required for equilibrium.

\end{exercise}

\end{exercise}

\subsubsection{Spring force}
Ideal strings do not exist. In fact, no physical material can be perfectly rigid and there is always some---perhaps tiny--- strechiness to them. 
Springs are the instrument in physics that can contribute forces by stretching/compressing. 

\midfig{IdealSpring}{25}{An ideal spring obeys Hooke's rule: It takes an external force $F_{ext} = k Δx$ to stretch the spring by an amount $Δx$. The spring exerts the opposite force $F_s=-kΔx$.}
A spring is ideal if it is massless, frictionless, and satisfies Hooke's rule\index{Hooke's rule}: The force $F_{sp}$ of a spring on an object is proportional to the amount $Δx$ that it has been stretched
$$
F_s = -k Δx
\quad \quad \textrm{where} \quad \quad 
[k] = \frac{M}{T²}
$$
The sign indicates that the direction of the force is {\em opposite} to the displacement of the spring from its resting length; it is an example of a {\em restoring force}\index{Force; restoring}.
Here $k$ is called the {\em spring constant}\index{spring constant}
and we have explicitly indicated its dimensions. It is a property of the spring. Being ideal, springs do not interfere in the dynamics and do not appear in FBDs.

\midfig{RampSpring}{50}{Incline with mass and spring of exercises \ref{E:RampSpring0} and \ref{E:RampSpring}.}

Hooke's rule is often called Hooke's Law, but it should not be, because it is barely a definition. Springs can be ideal in many ways and not be Hookian just because they don't follow the rule to the letter. For example, a spring can be pre-wound in which case a non-zero amount of force is required before it begins to stretch, just like in your spring lab. (After this initial force, it may behave more Hookian.) 
Other springs are Hookian when stretched ($Δ x >0$), but cannot be compressed ($Δ x<0$). 
In problem statements, you should assume a spring is Hookian unless the contrary is explicitly stated. Such springs stretch ($Δx >0$) just as easily as they compress ($Δx<k$). 

\begin{exercise}\label{E:RampSpring0}
A block of mass $M$ sits on a frictionless plane ($μ =0$) inclined at an angle $θ$. It is being kept from sliding further down the incline by a Hookian spring with spring constant $k$. Show that the compression of the spring is given by the formula
$$
Δx = \frac{Mg}k \sin θ 
$$
\end{exercise}

\subsection{Newton III}

Newton's Third Law of dynamics is a statement of reciprocity relating forces acting between two objects. It is often stated as some version of “for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction”. More precisely:
\paragraph{Newton III}
If an object $A$ acts on another object $B$ with a force $𝐅_{AB}$, then $B$ acts on $A$ with a force $𝐅_{BA}$ and 
$$
𝐅 _{BA} = - 𝐅_{AB}
$$

For example, if you push on a door, the door pushes back. The magnitude with which the door pushes back is equal to the magnitude of the force you are applying. If you slap a table with your hand, the table slaps your hand back and you feel that as a stinging sensation. 

Newton's Third Law may seem innocuous enough, but it is often used in vexing paradoxes of the type: If I push on a cart and the cart pushes back with the same force, how can it accelerate? Don't the forces cancel? The answer to this question is always “No, because the forces are acting on different objects, and forces acting on different objects cannot cancel.” When you push on the cart, the cart pushes back on your hand, but that doesn't change the fact that there is an unbalanced force on the cart; the cart will accelerate (provided the force you applied suffices to overcome any friction with the ground, ball bearings, and such). What can also happen though, is that when the cart pushes on you, you may also accelerate in the opposite direction of the cart's acceleration. What is stopping you from doing that is usually friction with the ground: The cart pushes on you, but friction also pushes on you, so if there is enough friction (which is what your rubber soles are providing), you will not accelerate. 

If, on the other hand, there is no friction with the floor, say because you are standing on ice, then you will--in fact--accelerate: One way to accelerate yourself when you are standing on ice is to push on something very massive. 

Suppose you, an object of mass $m$, are standing on a frictionless surface. Within reach is a large mass $M$ also on that frictionless surface. Suppose you push the large mass with a force of magnitude $F$ in an attempt to accelerate it away from you. That mass will indeed accelerate away from you in the amount $a_M= \frac FM$. However, by Newton's Third Law, that mass will apply a force on you of equal magnitude $F$, so you will accelerate too in the amount $a_m = \frac Fm$. Notice that if you are less massive than the other object, you will accelerate more: $a_m > a_M$.

\subsection{FBDs: The Free-Body Diagram Technique}\index{Free-Body Diagram, FBD}

The main tool for analyzing the dynamics of a system using Newton's three laws is the free-body diagram. We first describe the procedure and then demonstrate on some examples. 

\begin{enumerate}
\item For each mass in the problem, draw a solid dot. We will need to do many things for each dot, so make sure they are well-separated on the page. 
\item For each dot, draw an arrow {\em emanating from the dot} for each force acting on the corresponding mass.
\item For each dot, write a separate copy of Newton's second law formula $∑_i 𝐅_i = m {\bf a}$. The mass in this formula is the mass corresponding to the dot on which you are focusing. This is a vector equation, so we will need to reduce it to components in order to work with it.
\item \label{coords}
For each dot, choose a convenient system of coordinates and decompose the vector equation into components. For example, you could choose horizontal/vertical or, if an inclined plane is involved, along the plane/perpendicular to the plane. Pick which directions are positive and write the two component equations with all the correct signs. Note that you can choose these coordinates dot-by-dot: You do not need to commit to one system and apply it to every dot.
\item You now have a system of equations. There is a maximum of two equations for each dot and there are as many dots as there are masses in the problem. This gives enough information to solve for all the unknowns, so you should write symbolic equations for the dynamical variables (usually $a$ and $T$).
\item Reflect on the meaningfulness of your equations and test the limits. Is everything well-defined when some masses are equal? Do you get what you expect when you take masses to be large/small? Can you take angles to special values to recover previously-known formul\ae{}?
\item Plug numbers to get answers to the problem.
\end{enumerate}
Notice that you cannot check whether your answer is sensible in the penultimate step if you were plugging numbers from the start. 

\paragraph{A comment about Newton III} In the previous section, we formulated Newton's Third Law of dynamics. There we emphasized that the “action” force and “reaction” force act on different objects. What this means for FBDs is that {\em the two forces of a Newton III pair can never appear on the same dot in the diagram}. 

\paragraph{A (long) note about choosing coordinates} In step \ref{coords}, you are told to choose convenient coordinates, but what does that really mean? When there is only one mass in your FBD, it means just what it sounds like, but if there are multiple masses, how should we do it? To answer this question, let us notice something important about the fully labelled FBD with all its dots: Each dot has its own forces and Newton II rules, but some dynamical variables are shared across dots: The acceleration in one part will be related to that in another and/or the tension in appearing for one dot will be the same as that appearing in the another. 
These variables are what connect the equations across the entire diagram allowing you to solve for them. Because of this, the way you should choose your coordinates should be dictated by whatever makes these connections the simplest. 

To fully explain this, we need a complicated set-up. We won't solve it yet, but we will use it to explain what is meant here. Consider the example problem called “Ramp and pulley” in section \ref{S:RampPulley} below. Figure \ref{F:PulleyRamp} shows a block of mass $M$ on an inclined plane with angle $θ$ connected by an ideal pulley to a freely-hanging mass $m$. The two masses appear as separate dots in the FBD shown in figure \ref{F:FBD2}. The question is how to choose coordinates. For the second mass, this is obvious: The dynamics are entirely vertical so we pick a vertical/horizontal decomposition. My preference is to orient things so the accelerations are positive when you cut all wires and turn off all frictions, so for this I would pick downward as the positive direction (but you do you). (It is also my preference to let the simplest part of the FBD determine everything else, and this part is clearly simpler because the horizontal direction is irrelevant, so it reduces to 1D dynamics.) 

Next, we look at the larger mass $M$ where things are more complicated since it's on an incline. We could choose horizontal and vertical again, but there is a great reason to {\em not} do that: the string connecting them. This simple element--with the help of the pulley--connects this part of the FBD to the one for the hanging mass $m$, and it does so in a way that the acceleration of $M$ is related to that of $m$. This relation is only direct in the {\em direction upward along the ramp}. I cannot emphasize enough that these accelerations do not agree as vectors, since they point in different directions. We are cleverly {\em choosing directions} so that they will match magnitude-wise!

This connection between different parts of the diagram is so strong, that we predicate all consequent choices on it: Imagine what a mess you would make here were you to insist on vertical \& horizontal over along string/ramp \& orthogonal to it! 

{\em That} is how we choose axes.\footnote{Is it consistent? Yes: Each part of the FBD gets its own Newton II equation and its formulation relies only on an IRF. You can think of each part of the FBD as having its own IRF which are all at rest relative to each other but displaced and rotated. Displaced and rotated IRFs are still IRFs! Remember: Everything goes, as long as they are not accelerating relative to each other.}

\subsubsection{Pushing on blocks}
Consider an arrangement of blocks at right. We apply a force $F_{ext}= 20\ N$ to the smaller block. The smaller block, in turn, applies a force of magnitude $F_{mM}$ to the larger block. The larger block returns the favor according to Newton III, applying a force of equal magnitude $F_{Mm}=F_{mM}$ in the opposite direction. Let's figure out what happens. We take everything to be frictionless. 

The FBD for this problem consists of two dots (the blocks):
\midfig{Blocks1FBD}{50}{FBD for blocks}
The forces $F_{N,m}$ and $F_{N,M}$ are the normal forces for the two blocks. Since they are the only ones acting in the vertical direction, applying the vertical component of Newton II tells us that 
$F_{N,m} = mg$ and $F_{N,M} = Mg$. 

The force $F_{mM}$ applied by the smaller mass acts only the larger one, so it appears in the second part of the FBD only. The reciprocal force $F_{Mm}$ acts only on the smaller mass and appears, therefore, only in the first part of the diagram. These forces are equal in magnitude. The external force $F_{ext}$ acts only on the first mass, so it too appears in that part of the diagram. 

Applying the horizontal component of Newton II gives $F_{ext} - F_{Mm} = m a$ and $F_{mM} = Ma$.
Together, these give the acceleration as 
$$a = \frac{1}{m+M}F_{ext}$$ 
Plugging this back in, we also find that 
$$F_{mM} = F_{Mm} = \frac{M}{m+M}F_{ext}$$ 

\rightfig{StopLight}{65}{A stoplight hanging from an arrangement of three cables}


Now we reflect: The formula for the acceleration makes sense because you could really think of the two blocks together as one object of mass $m+M$. 
The formula for the (re)action force has the property that $F_{mM}→ 0$ when $M→ 0$. In this case, there is no second block, so the force of the first on should vanish. Similarly the second block cannot exert a force on the first if it is not there. 
A second limiting case has $F_{mM}→ F_{ext}$ when $m → 0$. This means the first block is not there, so $F_{ext}$ would act directly on the second box. In both cases, the system accelerates as if there is only one box, so the equation for the acceleration has  the appropriate limits as well.

\subsubsection{Atwood Machine}

\rightfig{Atwood}{35}{Atwood machine}
Consider two masses $m$ and $M$ connected by an ideal string stretched over two ideal pulleys as shown at right. The goal is to determine the acceleration $a$ of the system as well as the tension $T$ in the string. 
The FBD consists of two dots, each with an arrow for the weight and for the tension. If $M>m$ the mass on the left will accelerate downward. Since the mass on the right is connected by an ideal string (which, therefore, doesn't stretch) so it must accelerate upward at the same rate. 

The dots are disconnected parts of a single FBD, so we may choose this directions to call positive-increasing for each dot. We then write out the components of Newton's second law for each dot keeping careful track of the signs of the terms according to our chosen conventions for what is positive. 

In the case just described, Newton's second law is one-dimensional, so we get the following equations: For the large mass, 
$$
Mg - T = Ma
$$
because the + direction is downward. Note the on the RHS, the large mass appears. For the small mass, the upward direction is positive, so two of the three signs are switched:
$$
- mg + T = ma
$$
The sign on the RHS is correct because $m$ will accelerate upward with rate $a$ at the same time that $M$ will accelerate downward at rate $a$ (since they are connected by an ideal string).

As expected, this has given two equations for two unknowns. If we add the equations together, the tensions cancel, and we are left with 
\eq{\label{E:Atwooda}
Mg-mg =Ma +ma
\implies{⇒}
a = \frac{M-m}{M +m} g
}
To solve for the tension, it would be nice to take the difference to cancel the $a$s, but one has a coefficient of $m$ and the other $M$. We can get rid of these by dividing the first equation by $M$ to get 
$
Mg - T = Ma 
⇒
g -\frac TM  = a 
$
and the second by $m$ to get
$
- mg + T = ma
⇒ 
- g +\frac Tm  = a 
$
Subtracting gives
\eq{\label{E:AtwoodT}
0= 2g -\frac TM -\frac Tm = 2g - \frac{M+m}{mM}T
\implies{⇒}
T = 2 \frac{mM}{M+m} g
}

Now for the reflection part: 
\begin{itemize}
\item We notice that in the next-to-last step in solving for $a$, it appears with the prefactor $m+M$. This makes sense because this is the mass of the whole system, and it is the whole system that accelerates.
\item If $M=m$, then $a$ vanishes, as it should. When that happens, the tension balances the weight of each mass, and, indeed, we see that in that case $T→ mg$. This also explains the need for the “$2$” in the formula for $T$.
\item If $m=0$, then only $M$ is left, and it accelerates at $a=g$, that is, free fall. Also, $T→ 0$ which makes sense if there is no counter-mass to give the tension. 
\item The formula for $a$ is anti-symmetric under interchange of the masses. That is, if we switch $M \leftrightarrow m$, then $a$ switches sign. This makes sense because if $M$ turned out to be the smaller mass, then the system would accelerate in the opposite direction. Since we picked $a$ to be positive because we thought the system accelerated the other way, that makes sense too. Conversely, $T$ doesn't care which side is more massive: The ideal pulley guarantees the tension is the same on both sides. This is reflected in the fact that the formula for $T$ is symmetric under exchange of the masses. 
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Table and pulley}

\rightfig{PulleyTable}{45}{Table with masses connected by an ideal pulley}
%\begin{center}
%\includegraphics[height=50mm]{/Users/wdlinch3/Dropbox/NoVa/TJHSST/Teaching/Physics/ClassNotes/Figures/Forces/PulleyTable.png}
%\end{center}
Next, we put one of the masses on a table as shown at right. 
As always, the first thing to do is imagine the situation. If we set up this experiment and let go of the masses, we might think the big mass doesn't move and the small one doesn't either. However, if we imagine the table is {\em frictionless}, then that cannot be, because then there is no friction force on $M$ to balance the tension in the string. The system must accelerate with the small mass moving downward and the large one to the right. Since the string is ideal, the distance between the masses cannot change so the two must accelerate by the same amount (albeit in different directions). 

The free body diagram for this setup has two dots for $M$ and $m$, but the former has 4 arrows emanating from the dot (3 if you ignore friction) and the latter has two. Another difference is that the $M$ part is 2D now: We cannot avoid having to split Newton's Law into components. 

\midfig{FBDs}{50}{Free-body diagram for table with masses}

\paragraph{No friction} 
We begin with the simplified case in which we ignore friction.
Focusing on the $m$ dot first, we have only a vertical part to Newton II. Taking downward to be positive for this part of the diagram, Newton II says
$$
∑F_{vert} = ma
\implies{⇒}
mg - T = ma
$$
Notice that the $T$ arrow upward and the $mg$ downward cannot have the same length as this would imply that $m$ is not accelerating. 

For the $M$ part of the diagram, we have to split Newton II into horizontal and vertical parts. The vertical parts are old-hat: There is no acceleration into or off the table because the normal force $F_N$ balances the weight $Mg$:
$$
∑ F_{vert} = ma_{vert}
\implies{⇒}
Mg - F_N = 0
$$
On the other hand, there is only one force in the horizontal part:
$$
∑ F_{hor} = ma_{hor}
\implies{⇒}
T = Ma
$$
(Of course, we are choosing the positive direction to be to the right.)

This time we have 3 equations for 3 unknowns 
$$
Mg - F_N = 0
\quad, \quad
mg - T = ma
\quad, \quad
T = Ma
$$
Again we have solutions (check this!):
$$
F_N = Mg 
\quad, \quad
a = \frac m{M+m} g 
\quad, \quad
T = \frac {mM}{M+m} g 
$$
You should compare these answers to the ones above for the Atwood machine and think about the sensibility of the answers in each case.

\paragraph{The reduced mass}
If you are working hard to live up to your potential, you have checked these and may have noticed that $m+M$ appeared in front of $a$ again, just as before. You may also have noticed that the combination 
$$
m_{red} = \frac {mM}{M+m} 
$$
appeared again. This combination is called the “reduce mass”, and it will continue to appear in problems throughout physics.\index{Mass; reduced, reduced mass}
If you are good at algebra and an industrious student, I recommend you show that $m_{red} < m$ and $m_{red}<M$, so it is less than the smaller of the two masses. This also implies that $m_{red}→ 0$ if either mass vanishes. 
You should also check that $m_{red} → \frac m2$ when we take $M→ m$.

\paragraph{Adding friction}
We will now add friction. Suppose $M=5.0\ kg$, $m=3.0\ kg$, and $μ_s = 0.4$. Also, in these notes $g = 10.0\ m/s²$, because we are among friends. Now, is this system in static equilibrium?

This sounds like a hard problem, but it is really not difficult to answer, once you understand what to check: We will calculate the force of friction $F_{s,needed}$ that would be necessary for equilibrium, and then we will compare that to the maximum force of static friction $F_{s,max}$. The system will be static if $F_{s,needed} ≤ F_{s,max}$, and the force of static friction $F_s =F_{s,needed}$ will be the one we computed. However, if it turns out that $F_{s,needed} > F_{s,max}$, then we would need to exceed the maximum available static force, so the system will not be in equilibrium, and it will begin to accelerate.

Let's now carry out this plan: We suppose for the sake of argument that it is in equilibrium. Then neither mass is accelerating. For the small mass in particular, this means that the tension must balance the weight: $T= 30\ N$. Is this possible? For the large mass on the table, it would mean that the force of static friction is equal to the tension: $F_s = 30\ N$. This could fail to be possible, so let's check. We know the formula for the {\em maximum} force of static friction $F_{s,max}$. If $F_{s,max} ≧  30\ N$, everything is fine and the system is in equilibrium. 

However, we recall from section \ref{S:Friction} that $F_{s,max} = μ_s F_N$, so suddenly, we actually care what the normal force is. Since nothing changed in the vertical direction $F_N=Mg$ still, so $F_{s,max} = μ_s Mg = 0.4 × 50\ N = 20\ N$. This is less than the $30\ N$ we need, so the system cannot be in equilibrium.\footnote{This form of argument is called “proof by contradiction”: You start by pretending that the wrong answer is true, and you show that that leads to a contraction. In this case, we started by assuming the system was in equilibrium and we concluded that $30 < 20$ which is not true, {\it i.e.} a contradiction.}

OK! So we know that the system is accelerating because max static friction is not sufficient to keep it from doing so. The next logical step is to figure out the dynamics: Let's find $a$ and $T$ again!

Actually, we don't even really need to redraw our diagrams. The only things that change are
\begin{itemize}
\item Nothing for the small mass $m$: It was never in equilibrium and still is not. WARNING: The tension changes though, because the dynamics of the system as a whole are different. Friction will “slow down” the acceleration of $m$, so don't go plugging old values of $a$ and $T$ in for this part.
\item The large mass $M$ has a new contributor to its part of the FBD: Newton II becomes $T-F_k=Ma$.
\item The force of kinetic friction $F_k = μ_k F_N$, and the normal force was $F_N= Mg$, so $F_k = μ_k Mg$. 
\end{itemize}

Putting the last two observations together, we find that $T-μ_k Mg = Ma$. Combining this with the unchanged equation $mg - T = ma$ for the other mass, we find (verify!)
\eq{
\label{F:PulleyFrictionTable}
a = \frac{m -μ_kM}{M+m} g
\and{and}
T= (1+μ_k) \frac {mM}{M+m} g 
}


Again, we are not done: We have our equations, but the point of deriving them is to {\em learn/do some physics}. Firstly, if $μ_k→ 0$, we should recover the answers we already had, and we do. Secondly, we expect friction to reduce the acceleration, and the sign in the acceleration formula implies that it does just that. For the tension it looks like it adds, which also makes sense: more drag; more tension.\footnote{Finally, here is a curious thing: If we crank $μ_k→ 1$, we recover Atwood. Does that make sense??The answer is: In a weird way, yes. Kinetic friction is a curious thing because it “redirects” the normal force to go sideways: To get the sideways frictional force $F_k$ you start with the vertical normal force $F_N$, take the magnitude, and then multiply by a coefficient ($μ_k$). So in these equations, if you set $μ_k→ 1$ it's like setting $F_k → F_N$ {\em even though these are not in the same direction}. If you just pretend they are, then these are exactly the forces appearing in Atwood when you “rotate” the friction to be vertical.}

\subsubsection{Ramp and pulley}
\label{S:RampPulley}


%\begin{center}
%\includegraphics[height=50mm]{/Users/wdlinch3/Dropbox/NoVa/TJHSST/Teaching/Physics/ClassNotes/Figures/Forces/PulleyRamp.png}
%\end{center}
As our next complication, let's introduce some non-trivial geometry! Consider the generalization of our previous set-ups in which the table is replaced with an inclined plane.

Let us note from the outset that $θ =0$ corresponds to the table in the previous section, and $θ = 90^∘$ is the Atwood again. 
We will start with no friction and then ask the same kinds of questions as before. After you have derived your answers, you should check that you recover your previous results in the appropriate limits.

\rightfig{PulleyRamp}{35}{Inclined plane with masses and ideal pulley}

Let's set $θ = 30^∘$, and let's solve for the dynamics. 
The FBD is given in figure \ref{F:FBD2}, but to draw it, I had to guess at the direction of the force of friction. 
Let's see whether I guessed correctly by considering the frictionless case. 


The next step in the FBD algorithm is to apply Newton II in the form $𝐅_{net} = m\bf a$ to each part. 
For the small mass, the dynamics are non-trivial only in the vertical direction. If we take the downward direction to be positive, the net force is $∑F_{vert} = mg -T$ and Newton II reduces to 
$$
mg-T = ma
$$




For the large mass, we need to decompose the 2D vector equation into components. We may choose axes different from those for the small mass, {\em because this is an entirely different part of the diagram}. If we choose to decompose into vertical and horizontal directions, then only one vector (the weight) lines up with an axis. Also, if there is an acceleration up or down the ramp, it would have components in both directions and we would have to do more work.

\midfig{FBD2}{55}{Free-body diagram for inclined plane with masses and pulley}

A better choice is to decompose with respect to directions up the ramp (parallel or $\parallel$) and orthogonal (perpendicular or $⊥$) to the ramp:
$$
∑_i 𝐅_i = M{\bf a} ⟶ 
\begin{cases}
∑ F_\parallel =
	T- Mg\sin \theta = Ma & \textrm{parallel }  \\
∑ F_⊥=	
	F_N - Mg\cos \theta = 0  & \textrm{perpendicular } 
\end{cases}
$$
Note that I'm allowed to use the same $T$ and the same $a$ for both dots because firstly, the pulley is ideal, and secondly the string is ideal (so both masses must have the same acceleration). 

The perpendicular equation for $M$ just tells us the normal force is $F_N = Mg\cos \theta$. The parallel equation can be combined with the single vertical equation for $m$ to give 
$$
a = \frac{m-M\sin θ}{M+m} g
\and{and}
T=(1+\sin θ ) \frac{mM}{M+m} g
$$,

This was for the frictionless case. For the numbers we decided ($g=10\ m/s²$, $M= 5.0\ kg$, $m=3.0\ kg$, $θ = 30^∘$), we find $F_N= 43.\ N$, $a = 0.63\ m/s²$ and $T= 28.\ N$. 
Notice that $a>0$ so the small mass accelerates downward while the large one accelerates up the ramp at the same rate.
\begin{exercise} 
At which angle is the system in equilibrium?
\end{exercise}

When we include friction, this observation implies that the frictional force will be pointing down the ramp, just as we originally guessed. (Had the acceleration been negative, that would have meant that the large mass would slide down the ramp pulling the small mass upward. In that case, the frictional force would have been directed up the ramp.)

Next, we put $μ_s = 0.4$ and ask: “Is the system in equilibrium?” 
We prove that it is as follows: If it were not in equilibrium, then that would mean that the maximum possible frictional force 
$$
F_{s,max} = μ_s F_N = μ_s Mg \cos θ 
$$
does not suffice to keep the block from sliding. Is that true? 
Well, plugging in the numbers, we find for this case that $F_{s,max} = 17\ N$. 

How much do we need to keep the block in place? The system is static when the sum of the parallel forces vanishes:
$$
∑ F_{\parallel} = T - Mg\sin θ -F_s = M a= 0
\implies{⇒}
F_s = T - Mg\sin θ
$$
Since $m$ is also static in this situation, $T=mg$, so 
$$
F_s = mg - Mg\sin θ
$$
Plugging in the numbers, we find $F_s = 5.0\ N$ for the force of static friction that we would need to keep the system balanced. Since this is (comfortably) under the maximum of $17\ N$ we found above, the system is in equilibrium.
\begin{exercise}
For what range of angles will this system be in equilibrium? In other words, what are the maximum and minimum angles for which static friction balances out all the forces?
\end{exercise}

\subsection{Momentum and Impulse}
In the previous section, we mostly used Newton's Second Law in the form 
$$
∑_i 𝐅_i = m \bf a
$$
but recall that Newton originally formulated this law as relating the left-hand side to a change in the linear momentum (or “quantity of motion” as he put it):
$$
𝐅_{net} = \frac{Δ𝒑}{Δt} 
$$
Rearranging this as 
$$
Δ𝒑 = 𝐅_{net}\ Δt
$$
we can read it as saying that a change in momentum due to a force $𝐅_{net}$ is proportional to the new force applied and the amount of time over which it acts. 

This is quite intuitive: Suppose you want to push a friend in one of  the rolly chairs around the commons. To get a maximum change in momentum (from rest to $𝒑_{max}$) you do two things: Push hard and push long. If you push hard but not long enough, $Δ𝒑$ is not as big as you could make it. Conversely, if another friend who cannot push hard just pushes for a longer time, then $Δ𝒑$ will can get just as big or bigger. 

This combination of force applied and the time over which it is applied is called the {\em impulse}\index{impuse}. For a constant average force $𝐅_{ave}$ acting for a time $Δt$, the average impulse is\footnote{The $I$ is taken by the important notion of "current". Ironically, the current density will later we called $\bf j$. }
$$
𝑱_{ave} = 𝐅_{ave} Δt
$$
\begin{exercise}
A tennis player serves the ball perfectly. The ball has mass $m= 60\ g$ and the velocity of the ball after the serve is $v_f = 120\ km/hr$. The average force of the racket on the ball is $F_{ave} = 800\ N$. 
\begin{enumerate}
\item Calculate the change in momentum $Δp$ before and after the serve.
\item Calculate the time $Δt$ that the ball is in contact with the racket.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}


\begin{exercise}
Now suppose we are back to pushing our friends around in chairs. 
Your push consists of the following parts:
\begin{itemize}
\item For the first second, you are not pushing. 
\item For the next two seconds, you push with a force of $800 N$ 
\item For a second after that, you push with a force of $400 N$. 
\item For a next three seconds, you run alongside but don't push. 
\item You pull with a force of $600\ N$ for two seconds.
\end{itemize}
Let's calculate some impulses:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Graph the force as a function of time for this push.
\item Calculate the change in momentum for each of the parts separately. 
\item Calculate the total change in momentum in this push.
%Show that the total change in momentum over the total 3 second-long push is $Δ𝒑 = 1200 Ns$.
\item Relate your calculations to the area under the $F$-$t$ graph. 
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}
This exercise shows that it makes sense to define the impulse for a force that is not constant in time, and that the impulse with be the area under the force {\it v.s.}\ time graph counted with signs. (Recall that this means the areas under the $t$ axis count as negative.)\footnote{If you noticed this sleight of hand, good for you! The force is a vector, so defining the “area under the graph” requires some clarification. In the event that it matters, split the forces into components and apply the area definition of impulse for each component. Since each component $F^i(t)$ (for $i=x,y,z$) will give a different area, we get three different impulse components $J^i$. We then put the components back together into an impulse vector $𝑱$.}
%$$
%J = \textrm{area under }F\textrm{-}t\textrm{ graph}
%$$
\begin{definition}[Impulse]
When a force $𝐅$ (not necessarily constant) acts on an object over a time interval $Δt$, the {\em impulse} it imparts on the object is defined to be the (signed) area under the $F$-$t$ graph. When the force is constant, the impulse is reduced to the product $𝐅Δt$ of the force and the time over which it acts. 
\end{definition}

The relation between this concept and the change in momentum of an object is called the {\em impulse-momentum theorem}:
%%%
\begin{theorem}[Impulse-momentum]
When a net force $𝐅_{net}$ acts on an object, the change in momentum $Δ𝒑$ of that object is given by the net impulse ({\it i.e.}\ that imparted by the net force).
\end{theorem}
%%%
The proof of this “theorem” is essentially an application of the rules of calculus to Newton's Second Law. 
\begin{exercise}

\end{exercise}

\end{exercise}

\end{exercise}

\subsubsection{Conservation of momentum}
Newton's first law states that if there is no force acting on an object in an inertial frame of reference, then the linear momentum of that object will be constant. 

\begin{exercise}
Imagine that you are floating in outer space with no gravitational or other influences acting on you with some net force. Suppose that you are holding a bowling ball of mass $m= 7 kg$. Your spacesuit is very bulky and massive, so your mass is $m=210 kg$. 
Suppose you push the bowling ball away from you with a force of $160\ N$ in a time $Δt = 1.5\ s$. 
\begin{enumerate}
\item Now suppose you have thrown the ball. Calculate the impulse you impart on it. 
\item According to an observer at rest with respect to you, what are the momenta of you and the ball? What is the sum of these momenta?
\item According to the inertial observer, what is the momentum of the ball upon release?
%\item Calculate the final speed of the ball.
\item Calculate the impulse the ball imparts on you. 
\item According to the inertial observer, what is your momentum upon release? 
%\item Calculate your final speed.
\item According to the inertial observer, what is the change in momentum from before the ball was thrown to after? Why is this happening?
\item Calculate the final velocity of you and the ball according to the inertial observer. 
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

In this problem the final momenta add up to the same amount as the initial momenta did (in this case $𝟎$). This happened because there were no external net forces acting on the ball/astronaut system. There {\em were \underline{internal}} forces acting on the parts, but by Newton's Third Law, their impulses were equal in size and opposite in direction. 

\begin{definition}[Isolated system]
A collection of objects with no net external forces acting on it is called an {\em isolated system}.\index{isolated system}
\end{definition}

The conclusion drawn above would hold even if the inertial observer were moving relative to you and the ball at a constant velocity. The only difference is that the momenta would add up to the total mass multiplied by this constant velocity. 
In general, we have the statement
\begin{theorem}[Conservation of (linear) momentum]
In an inertial frame of reference the total linear momentum of an isolated system is conserved. 
\end{theorem}
By total linear momentum is meant the sum of the momenta of all the parts. By “conserved” is meant that it does not change. 

%\begin{exercise}
%There is a particle $W$ with mass $m_W = 1.43 × 10⁻²⁵\ kg$ that decays to an electron $e$ with mass $m_e=9.11 × 10⁻³¹\ kg$ and a neutrino $ν$ with a mass $m_ν = 1.25 × 10⁻³⁷\ kg$. Suppose the $W$ is at rest in the frame of reference of the lab and the electron emerges with a velocity of $3.00 × 10⁻⁷\ \frac ms$ heading due East, what is the velocity of the neutrino?
%\end{exercise}

\begin{example}
A cart of mass $M = 10 \ kg$ rolls on a frictionless track at a constant speed $v_i=6 \frac ms$ until it collides with a cart of mass $m = 5 kg$ and sticks to it. Calculate the final velocity of the stuck-together carts. 

The solution is found by recognizing that the two carts form an isolated system so the total momentum is conserved. Since the second cart is at rest, the initial momentum is $p_i = Mv_i + m \cdot 0 = 60\ \frac{kg\cdot m}s$. This must equal the final momentum. After the carts collide, they stick together so whatever the velocity of one of them is, that must also be the velocity of the other. Let's call it $v_f$. Then the final momentum of the stuck-together carts is $p_f = (M+m)v_f = 15 v_f$. Conservation requires $p_f=p_i$, so $15 v_f = 60\ \frac{m}s$, so $v_f = 4\ \frac ms$.
\end{example}

\rightfig{ImpulseEnergy/Bullet&Block}{55}{Bullet striking block; a completely inelastic collision}

\begin{exercise} 
\label{X:BulletBlock}

The block is originally resting on frictionless ice, but after absorbing the bullet, it slides over a rough surface with coefficient of kinetic friction $μ_k$ for a distance of $82\ cm$ before coming to rest.
\begin{enumerate}

In summary, when the force is constant, then its components are too, and we have the following statement: If a constant net force $𝐅_{net}$ acts on a particle over a displacement $Δ𝒓$, then the kinetic energy of the particle is changed according to 
$$
K_f - K_i = 𝐅_{net}∙Δ𝒓 
$$

This exercise shows us how to define the work in 3D: It consists of the dot product of the force vector and the displacement vector. 
\begin{definition}
When a constant force $𝐅$ is applied to an object that is displaced by $Δ𝒓$, the work done by $𝐅$ on the object is defined as 
\al{
W &= 𝐅 ∙ Δ𝒓 \cr
	&= F_x Δx +F_y Δy +F_z Δz 
}
\end{definition}
Note that it is crucial that the force be along the direction of displacement: There are no terms in this sum of the form $F_z Δx$. 
\begin{exercise}

\end{exercise}

\begin{example}
Suppose a person of mass $70\ kg$ walks up a flight of stairs a height $h=4\ m$. How much work does gravity do in this process? If the person is walking at a constant speed, how much work do they do in this process?

Notice that the horizontal displacement was not given in this problem, {\em but it is irrelevant} since gravity only points downward: The work done by gravity is $W_{grav} = 𝐅_g ∙ Δ{\bf r} = mg Δy= - mgh$. Since the person is walking at a constant velocity, the net work is zero. Therefore they did $mgh = 70 × 10× 4 = 2.8\ kJ$ of work. 
\end{example}


Again, we will extend the definition of work so that it applies to the appropriate area-under-the-curve concept. 
\begin{definition}(Work)
Consider a force $𝐅$ applied to an object that is displaced by $Δ 𝒓$. The work done by $𝐅$ on the object is defined as the sum of the areas under the respective component graphs of $F_x$ {\it vs.} $Δx$, $F_y$ {\it vs.} $Δy$, and $F_z$ {\it vs.} $Δz$.
\end{definition}
Notice that this is done component-by-component. With this understood, the concept of work can be applied to general, changing forces, like the Hooke force.

\label{Th:WKE}
Suppose a net work $𝐅_{net}$ acts on a particle. Then the change in the kinetic energy of that particle is given by the net work. 
\end{theorem}


\begin{example}

\end{example}

Happy New Year! \\
Let us review what we had been discussing before the break. \\
We had already defined the linear momentum $𝒑$ which appears in Newton's second law
$$
𝐅_{net} = \frac{Δ𝒑}{Δt} 
$$
For a particle of constant mass $m$, the momentum can be written as
$$
𝒑 = m 𝒗 
$$
in terms of the velocity $𝒗$ of the particle. When the net force is constant, we can rearrange the formula to read 
$$
 {Δ𝒑} = 𝐅_{net}{Δt}  = 𝑱_{net}
$$
where $𝑱_{net} =  𝐅_{net}{Δt}$ is called the net impulse. 
The units of impulse don't have a special name. In SI they can be written as $N \cdot s$ or $\frac{kg\ m}s$.
In general any number of forces can act on a system and each of them contributes their own impulse, but it is the {\em net} impulse that results in a change in the momentum of a system. This is the impulse-momentum theorem, and it holds also for non-constant forces if we generalize the impulse to mean the area under the force-time graph. 

The impulse momentum theorem for constant forces can also be derived by multiplying CAKE 1 by the mass and rewriting the resulting formula. If instead, we multiply CAKE 3 by the mass and rearrange, we get the analogous formula
$$
 {ΔK} = F_{net}{Δx}  = W_{net}
$$
where $W_{net} = F_{net}{Δx}$ is called the net work and 
$$
K = \frac12 m v² = \frac{p²}{2m}
$$
is called the kinetic energy of the particle. The unit of energy does get a special name: It is called the Joule. One Joule $J$ is equivalent to one Newton-meter $N \cdot m = \frac{kg\ m²}{s²}$. 

The relation above is called the work-kinetic energy theorem. 
Again we can generalize this to non-constant forces and to 3 dimensions. In three dimensions, we need to replace the net work with 
$$
W = 𝐅∙{Δ\bf r} = F_x\ Δx +F_y\ Δy +F_z\ Δz = F Δr \cos θ 
$$
The force $𝐅$ and the displacement ${Δ\bf r}$ are both vectors, and we found that we needed to combine them with the dot product (a.k.a. inner product or scalar product). In the second equality, we have written out this product in components, and in the third we have written it in terms of the magnitudes of the vectors and the cosine of the angle between them. 

There are (at least) two crucial things to remember:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Only the component of a force along the direction of displacement does work, so when you are carrying groceries down the street in a bag hanging from your arm, gravity is doing no work since the displacement is horizontal. 
\item The work that appears in the work-kinetic energy theorem is the {\em net} work. Plenty of forces can be doing work on a system, but it is the net work that determines whether there is a change in the kinetic energy.
\end{enumerate}


We can use the concept of work to define other forms of energy. Elastic energy is an example and the Hookian spring compressed an amount $x$ can do an amount of work
$$
W_{spring} = \frac12 k x² 
$$
where $k$ (in ${kg}/{s²}$) is the spring constant.

\subsubsection{Conservative and Non-conservative Forces}

In example \ref{X:Loop}, we used the work-kinetic energy theorem $W_{net} = ΔK$ to calculate the speed of a cart that undergoes a complicated roller-coaster motion without having to do any complicated calculus. This is pretty great, but it would be even nicer if we could somehow replace the net work with the change of some function, say $-ΔU$, because then we get $-Δ U = Δ K$ and the combination $K+U$ would be unchanged in the process. In fact, we can do that in this problem because we can take the function $U$ to be $mgh$. Then $K_i+U_i = mgh$ and $K_f+U_f = \frac12 mv²_f$, and we get the solution we derived.

This {\em almost} works, but exercise \ref{Ex:NonConservative} showed us that, in general, the work done by a force in some process depends on the path taken in that process: The work done in that exercise adds up to $500\ J$ even though we went in a loop. The overall displacement added up to 0, but we are adding force times displacement and that came out to $100\ J + 150\ J + 100\ J +150\ J = 500\ J$. For every part we could compute, $𝐅 ∙ Δ{\bf r}$ had the same sign and nothing canceled.

In contrast to this, the work done by gravity vanishes for any process in which the final and initial points have the same height. In exercise \ref{Ex:Conservative}, you repeated the loop of exercise \ref{Ex:NonConservative}, but got that the answer was $0\ J$. This time, the signs in $𝐅 ∙ Δ{\bf r}$ were different and the balance worked out to $0\ J - 150\ J + 0\ J +150\ J =0\ J$ for the work done by gravity. The 0s come about because the force of gravity was orthogonal to the displacement along these legs and the relative sign appeared because gravity always points down and the displacements in these legs were opposite.

Thus work is generally a function of the path taken, but for some forces it does not actually depend on the path. 
%We might even consider writing it as such $W(C)$ where $C$ is the  
We codify this distinction with a definition:
\begin{definition}
A force is called {\em conservative} if the work done by it in any process is independent of the path taken.
A force that is not conservative is called {\em non-conservative}.
\end{definition}
Notice that this is a bit meta: We are classifying forces by defining the work $W({\mathscr P})$ that force does along a path ${\mathscr P}$ and then checking whether it changes if we change the path to another path ${\mathscr P}'$: For any pair of paths ${\mathscr P}$ and ${\mathscr P}'$
\al{
\quad W({\mathscr P}') &= W({\mathscr P}) \quad \quad \textrm{force is conservative}
\cr
W({\mathscr P}') &≠  W({\mathscr P}) \quad \quad \textrm{force is non-conservative}
}

\begin{exercise} 
Argue that an equivalent definition would be to say that the force is conservative if the work around any closed loop is zero.
\end{exercise}

We make this distinction because the function $U$ that we want exists for conservative forces, but does not for non-conservative forces:
\begin{theorem}
\label{Th:PotentialEnergy}
Let $𝐅$ be a conservative force acting on an object that is displaced from a point $p_i$ to another point $p_f$. Then there is a function $U$ depending only on position for which the work done by $𝐅$ can be written as 
$$
W = U(p_i) - U(p_f) = -ΔU
$$
That is, the work is the difference between the value of $U$ at the initial point and that at the final point. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}\footnote{Please feel free to skip over this proof. Although we will use the result extensively, understanding why the claim is true is not crucial to using it.}
%The proof of this statement goes like this: 
Pick any random point $p_*$ you like--call it the base point--and define a function $U(p)$ to be the work the force $𝐅$ does in going from $p$ to $p_*$. Since the force is conservative, it doesn't matter how you get from $p$ to $p_*$, so this gives an honest function for every point $p$. (Below we will check that it is OK that this definition of $U$ depends on the base point.)

Now consider the work done in going from $p_i$ to $p_f$. Since the force is conservative, we may as well start from $p_i$ and go to the base point $p_*$ before continuing on from $p_*$ to $p_f$. Now the work done in going from $p_*$ to $p_f$ is equal in magnitude and opposite in sign from the work done in going from $p_f$ to $p_*$. (If you doubt this, do both processes successively to end up with a loop. Since the force is conservative the sum is 0 so each part cancels the other.)
Thus the work done in this process is precisely $W = U(p_i) - U(p_f)$.

Finally, we should make sure that this process does not depend on the random base point $p_*$ we picked. To do so, suppose we start over again with a different base point $p'_*$ and repeat all the steps with the new function $U'$. Then $U'(p)$ differs from $U(p)$ by a constant $c$ that is the work done by the force in going from $p'_*$ to $p_*$: $U'(p) = U(p) + c$. But this means that the work done in going from the initial point to the final point as calculated with this new function $U'$ will be
$$
W' = U'(p_i) - U'(p_f) = U(p_i) + c - U(p_f) -c  = U(p_i) - U(p_f)= W
$$
But this is the same as what we had before, so we have shown that the work done by the conservative force in going from $p_i$ to $p_f$ does not depend on $p_*$ or $p'_*$. 
\end{proof}
This proof actually shows that there is an infinite number of possible functions $U$ that all differ from one another by constant amounts. This ambiguity in the definition of the function corresponds precisely to the freedom of choosing the arbitrary base point used to define the function.
\begin{definition}
A function related to the work $W$ done by a conservative force as per the theorem above is called a {\em potential energy function}. Such functions are determined only up to an additive constant which cancels out of the defining relation 
$$
W= - ΔU 
$$
because $ΔU = U_f - U_i$ does not change if we shift $U$ by a constant. 
\end{definition}

\begin{exercise}
Consider a book of mass $m = 2\ kg$ resting on a bench of height $h = 90\ cm$. 
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Construct a potential energy function $U_g$ for the work done by the gravitational force in raising the book a distance $z$ above the table. 
	\item Demonstrate that $U'_g = U_g + c$ is also a valid potential energy function for any constant $c$. 
	\item Interpret the meaning of $c$. Hint: What choices did you make when you defined $U_g$?
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}

\end{exercise}


To recapitulate, any type of force can be classified as either conservative or non-conservative. All conservative forces have potential energy functions, whereas non-conservative forces cannot have such functions because they depend on paths instead of just starting and ending points. 

\paragraph{The Work-Energy Theorem}
We will now use all of this machinery to improve on our existing work-kinetic energy theorem.

Since any force is either conservative or not, the net work done $W_{net}$ in any process can be split into conservative $W_C$ and non-conservative $W_{NC}$ parts, and the conservative parts can be written in terms of a potential energy function $W_C =-Δ U$ by theorem \ref{Th:PotentialEnergy}. Writing this in formul\ae{},
$$
W_{net} = W_{NC}+W_C = W_{NC}-ΔU
$$
On the other hand, the work-kinetic energy theorem (theorem \ref{Th:WKE}) relates the LHS to the change in kinetic energy $W_{net} = ΔK$. Therefore, the non-conservative work changes the sum of the kinetic and potential energies:
$$
W_{NC}= Δ K + ΔU = Δ (K+U)
$$
The combination $K+U$ we were looking for will be important, so we give it a name:
\begin{definition}
The sum of the kinetic and potential energies is called the {\em mechanical energy} $E$.
$$
E = K + ∑_i U_i
$$ 
\end{definition}
Note that the potential energy $U=∑_i U_i$ may itself be a sum if there is more than one conservative force acting.

In general the non-conservative forces cannot be ignored, but we can still rewrite our work-kinetic energy theorem in a useful way:
\begin{theorem}[Work-Energy Theorem]
The change in mechanical energy $E = K + ∑_i U_i$ in any physical process for any system is given by the work done by all the non-conservative forces acting on the system:
$$
W_{NC} = ΔE
$$
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
This is just an interpretation, summary, and rewriting of the results we derived above. 
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
In a system in which all forces are conservative, the mechanical energy is conserved. In other words, in any process in which there are no non-conservative forces, the sum of the kinetic and all the potential energies in a system is the same initially as it is afterwards.
\end{corollary}

\subsubsection{Principle of Conservation of Energy}

Conservative forces are special in that the work done by them can be written as a change in a function $U$ the potential energy function associated to that force: $W_C = -ΔU$. 
Being a function, the potential energy only depends on points, so the work done will be a difference in the value of this functions at the starting and ending point of whatever process we are considering. 

Non-conservative forces do not have such a function, because they depend on the entire path along which a force acts, and not just on the end points of the path. Because functions depend on points, non-conservative forces cannot give rise to work that is the difference of a function between two points. Nevertheless, it is possible to write the work done by non-conservative forces as the difference in energy before and after the process. This energy will not be mechanical energy. Instead, it will be the energy associated to some internal change in the system; we call it internal energy:
$$
W_{NC} = -ΔE_{int} = E_{int, i} - E_{int, f}
$$
We emphasize that $E_{int, i}$ and $E_{int, f}$ are constants with the units of Joules, {\em they are not functions}. 

The use of introducing this concept is that now we can re-write the work-energy theorem as the conservation of total energy:
\begin{theorem}
The total energy of an isolated system is conserved. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Split the forces as conservative and non-conservative as before, but write the non-conservative part as above. Then the work-energy theorem becomes $ΔE_{int} = - W_{NC} = -ΔK - ΔU$.
Moving all terms to the right, we can rearrange this to $Δ(K+U+E_{int}) = 0$. Therefore, the total energy $K+U+E_{int}$ is constant. 
\end{proof}

This statement is often referred to as the Principle of Conservation of Energy. It states that energy cannot be created or destroyed. Rather, energy can only be changed from one form to another. Examples of the form the different parts of the energy can take are gravitational, elastic ({\it e.g.}\ spring), kinetic, thermal, nuclear, electric, and solar. This is not an exhaustive list, and it is possible that we do not yet know all the forms that energy can take.

It is also important to understand that not all forms of energy are interchangeable. For example, the work done by friction often results in a change in thermal energy: Rubbing your hands together makes them feel warm. That thermal energy cannot then be used to compress a spring. Heat is the name for changes in thermal energy, and there is a limit to how much useful work can be extracted from heat. 
The study of heat and its relation to mechanical energy is the theory of thermodynamics.

\subsection{Circular Motion}

Consider an object moving in a circle with constant speed $v$. Its velocity vector $𝒗$ is tangential to the circle in the direction of motion. It has constant length $v$, but its direction is continually changing. Because of this, the acceleration ${\bf a} = \frac{Δ𝒗 }{Δt}$ is non-zero even though the object is not speeding up or slowing down. 

Consider the difference in the velocity vector between an initial time $t_i$ and $t_f= t_i + Δt$. The speeds $v_f=v_i$ but $𝒗_f$ is rotated slightly relative to $𝒗_i$. Taking the difference $Δ𝒗 = 𝒗_f + (-𝒗_i)$, we see that it points radially inward. Since it does not matter which $t_i$ we started with, the acceleration points radially inward at all times. Such an acceleration is called {\em centripetal}, meaning “toward the center”. 

To get the magnitude, we note that the triangle formed by $𝒗_i$, $𝒗_f$, and $Δ𝒗$ is an isosceles triangle with sides $v$ and $Δv$. This triangle is formed by sweeping $𝒗_i$ through an angle $Δθ$. A similar triangle is formed by sweeping the radius vector through the angle $Δθ$. It has equal sides of length $r$ and a base that is very close to the arc $Δℓ = r Δθ$.
If the angle is very small, we may approximate this base by $Δℓ$. Since space and velocity triangles are similar, the sides satisfy 
$$
\frac{Δv}v = \frac{Δℓ}r 
\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad
Δv = \frac vr Δℓ
$$

Since we are considering circular motion, $r$ is constant in time. Since the motion is uniform $v$ is constant too. This means that the only thing changing on the RHS is $Δℓ$. Therefore, the acceleration has magnitude
$$
a = \frac{Δv}{Δt} = \frac vr \frac{Δℓ}{Δt}
$$
But $\frac{Δℓ}{Δt}$ is precisely the speed of the particle as it traverses the arc $Δℓ$, so we have derived that the magnitude of the centripetal acceleration is 
\eq{\label{E:centripetal}
\boxed{a_c = \frac{v²}{r} }
}

We emphasize this result with a box: In {\em any} motion in which an object follows the arc of a circle, there {\em must} be an acceleration directed into the center of the circle with magnitude given by \eqref{E:centripetal}. After all, if there were no acceleration, then the object is required to continue with constant velocity, so it must keep going in the same direction. 

Note that in circular motion the velocity is always tangent to the circle whereas the acceleration is always orthogonal to that. This means that the displacement is orthogonal to the centripetal acceleration. We will return to this point below when we consider the forces responsible for this centripetal acceleration. 

So far, we have considered circular motion in which the speed of the particle was constant. This “uniform” circular motion has a well-defined {\em period} of time $T$ it takes to make one revolution. The reciprocal $f=\frac1T$ of this period is called the {\em frequency}; its units are reciprocal seconds, also known as “Hertz”. 

When the frequency is expressed in terms of radians per second, it is called the {\em angular frequency} denoted $ω$ (omega):
$$
ω = \frac {2π}T = 2π f
$$
In terms of angular motion, this variable is the time rate of change of the angle, so another way of writing it is 
$$
ω = \frac{Δθ}{Δt}
$$
This form is more general since it is valid also for motion which does not consist of a single revolution. 

\begin{exercise}
Show that the angular velocity $ω$ for a particle in uniform circular motion of radius $r$ and speed $v$ satisfies
$$
ω = \frac vr
\quad \quad ⇔  \quad \quad
\boxed{v=r ω }
$$
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Rolling without slipping}

Consider now the motion of a wheel of radius $r$ rolling uniformly along a level surface without sliding. This is an example of uniform circular motion. The time it takes the wheel to make one revolution is the period $T$. Its angular speed is $ω =\frac {2π}T$, so a point on the edge of the wheel is moving relative to the fixed axis at a speed $v=rω$. 

Since the wheel is not slipping, this point must have zero relative velocity with respect to the ground. This means the axel must have speed 
$$
\boxed{v=rω}
$$ 
relative to the ground. In other words, a wheel that is rolling without slipping has translational velocity relative to the ground with magnitude $v=rω$. 

In this story, it is important that the motion of the particles on the edge of the wheel is circular motion: This is what introduces the geometry relating the translation to the rotation. However, at no point was it relevant that the motion was {\em uniform}. A wheel that is rolling without slipping can accelerate provided the translational acceleration is accompanied by an angular acceleration that guarantees the wheel is not slipping.

In the previous exercise, we generalized to the situation in which the motion is circular, but the angular velocity is not required to be constant. 
Analogously to the acceleration, we defined the {\em angular acceleration} to be the rate of change of the angular velocity.

 Since the motion is circular, the velocity vector of the particle will change direction just as it had for uniform circular motion, but its length will change in time. Define the tangential acceleration

to be the rate of change of the {\em speed} of the particle in circular motion.

\end{exercise} 

The {\em total acceleration} is the vector sum of the centripetal and tangential component vectors. By geometry, these are always perpendicular to one another. Therefore, circular motion that is not necessarily uniform, the magnitude of the acceleration is given by 
$$
a =√{a²_c + a²_t}
$$

\begin{example} 
A coin sits on a static turntable at a distance $r=9\ cm$ from the center. The coefficient of static friction is $μ_s = 0.67$. The turntable is switched on accelerating at an angular acceleration of $α = 1.23\ \frac{rad}{s²}$. At what time $t$ does the coin begin to slide?
\begin{solution}
The angular velocity is $ω = αt$, so the centripetal component of the acceleration is $a_c= rω² = r α² t²$ whereas the translational one is $a_t = r α$. The total acceleration has magnitude-squared $a² = a²_c + a²_t = r² α⁴ t⁴ + r² α²$. The maximum force that static friction can apply is $μ_s mg$ which will be equal to $ma$. Thus $μ_s g = a$ or
$$
μ²_s g² = r² α⁴ t⁴ + r² α²
\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad 
t⁴ = \frac{μ²_s g² -r² α² }{ r² α⁴ } ≈ 22.6 s⁴ 
$$
or, taking the fourth root, $t≈ 2.2\ s$.
\end{solution}
\end{example}

\begin{example} 
A car accelerates uniformly from rest around a circular road with radius $r$. 
\begin{enumerate}
	\item At what angle from its starting point does the centripetal component of its acceleration equal the radial component?
	\item At what time does this happen?
\end{enumerate}
\begin{solution}
Starting from rest, $ω_i =0$ so $a_c$ is initially 0 so its acceleration is entirely tangential. Since it is accelerating uniformly, its this component is $a_t = rα$. Over time, the angular speed is increasing according to $ω = α t$ so the centripetal component grows. As a function of angle $ω² = 2α Δθ$, so the centripetal component of the acceleration is growing as $a_c= rω² = 2rα Δθ$. The ratio
$$
\frac{a_c}{a_t} = \frac {2rα Δθ}{rα} = 2Δθ
$$
Therefore, the centripetal component begins to exceed the tangential one already at $Δθ =\frac12$ radians, or $Δθ ≈ 29^∘$.
In terms of time, $\frac{a_c}{a_t} = \frac {rα² t²}{rα} = αt²$, so this corresponds to at time $t=\frac1{√α}$.
\end{solution}
\end{example}
You will notice from this example, that in most cases, $a_t$ is negligible compared to $a_c$. When that is true, then $a=√{a_c² +a_r²} ≈ a_c$. In terms of answering questions about the acceleration on tests, be weary of any motion with relatively small angles (like $29^∘$), short times, and/or lots of significant figures.

\paragraph{The right-hand rule}
It turns out that working with oriented planes is often somewhat cumbersome, but in three spacial dimensions, there is a trick that can be used to assign a vector to every such plane and {\it vice versa}. 

Consider again the bench with a rotation in that plane around a point that is the origin of a polar coordinate system. In three dimensions, there is a unique line orthogonal to the plane of the bench that passes through the origin. This shows that to every rotation, we can uniquely assign a line. To assign a vector, we need to pick a direction on this line either into the plane of the bench or out of the plane of the bench. The convention for a counter-clockwise rotation is to pick the direction pointing out of the bench. 

This direction agrees with the following ritual: Using your open right hand, curl your fingers so that they describe the rotation. In this case, you can think of putting your right fist on the bench with your curled fingers rotating counter-clockwise. If you now extend your thumb, it will be pointing out of the bench in the same direction as the vector we just chose to describe the rotation.\footnote{If you had accidentally used your left hand, your fist would originally have had to be “up-side down” and your thumb would end up pointing into the bench instead. This is a perfectly acceptable convention as well, but it is not the one chosen historically.}

You can do the opposite as well: If you have a vector, say the displacement vector from your position to the exit door of the room, you can take your right hand, make a fist and line the direction of your thumb up with that vector. Then the base of your fist is flush with a plane orthogonal to that vector and oriented in the direction of your curled up fingers. As seen from the door, the plane is oriented counter-clockwise, as before. 

Now consider two vectors $𝒖$ and $𝒗$. If they are parallel, they describe a line and there must be some scalar $α$ such that $𝒖=α 𝒗$. If they are not parallel, then there is a plane in which they both lie. Putting $𝒖$ and $𝒗$ with their tails at the same point, we can apply the right-hand rule (RHR) to find a new vector describing the plane: Take your right hand and extend your fingers in the direction of the first vector $𝒖$. Then rotate your hand to whatever position you need it to be in so that when you curl your fingers, they point toward the direction that the second vector $𝒗$ is pointing. (This takes some practice; the instinct is to try to force the vectors to your hand's position, but you much move yourself so that you are oriented however the vectors dictate instead.) Once you have your (right!) hand in the correct position, your fist will be describing the oriented plane and your thumb will be pointing in the direction associated with that orientation. 

\begin{example} 
Suppose $𝒖$ is the displacement vector from you to the exit door and $𝒗$ is the displacement vector from you to the teacher's desk. Then the vector defined by the right-hand rule points directly upward from where you are sitting. 
\end{example} 

\begin{definition}
Let $𝒖$ and $𝒗$ be 3-dimensional vectors as before. We define the {\em cross product} $𝒖 × 𝒗$ to be that vector pointing in the direction defined by applying the RHR to $𝒖$ followed by $𝒗$ (in that order) with magnitude given by $||𝒖 × 𝒗|| = u v \sin θ$ where $θ$ is the angle between the vectors $𝒖$ and $𝒗$ in the plane spanned by them.
\end{definition}

\subsubsection{Rotational inertia from kinetic energy}
Consider again the particle in circular motion with radius $r$ and angular velocity $ω$. The tangential velocity of the particle is $v=r ω$. Recall that when we studied kinetic energy, it was not important whether the motion was uniform or not, circular or otherwise. Thus, the kinetic energy of this particle is, as it always has been $K=\frac12 m v²$. According to our analogy roadmap table \ref{T:Roadmap}, the analog of inertial mass is rotational inertia $I$. The analog of linear velocity is angular velocity, so the rotational analog of kinetic energy should be 
$$
K=\frac12 I ω². 
$$
Kinetic energy is kinetic energy irrespective of the motion, so this must agree with our previous expression. Expanding out $K=\frac12 m v²=\frac12 m(rω)²=\frac12 (mr²)ω²$, we find that the rotational inertia of a particle in circular motion is 
$$
I_{particle} = mr² 
$$
If we have a bunch of particles, say $N$ of them, with masses $m_i$ all rotating at constant distances $r_i$ from a common axis of rotation, then their inertia all add to give
\eq{\label{E:I}
I = ∑_{i=1}^N m_i r_i² 
}

For example a hoop of total mass $M$ is just a bunch of particles with masses $m$ adding up to $M=∑ m$ all at a distance $R$ away from the center and that therefore 
$$
I_{hoop} = MR² 
$$
as well. Furthermore, we argued that by dimensional analysis any other distribution of mass $M$ defined by a maximum length $R$ had to have the same $MR²$ form but with a coefficient that was smaller than 1. This is because any mass not at the maximum distance will have $r<R$ so these terms contribute less to the sum \eqref{E:I}. 
It is convenient to summarize this by saying that there must be some $ε$ for which 
$$
I_ε = ε MR² 
$$

Some examples are: 
\begin{description}
	\item[particle/hoop] A mass $M$ distributed entirely around the constant radius $R$ has $ε =1$.
	\item[disk/cylinder] A mass $M$ distributed as a uniform solid disk or cylinder of radius $R$ has $ε =\frac12$. Note that it does not matter how thick/tall the disk/cylinder is.
	\item[ball] A (solid) ball of mass $M$ and radius $R$ (like a bowling ball or marble) has $ε = \frac25$.
	\item[sphere] A (hollow) sphere of mass $M$ and radius $R$ (like a ping-pong ball or basket ball) has $ε = \frac23$.
	\item[rod] A rod of length $R$ and mass $M$ has $ε =\frac1{12}$ when rotated through its center but $ε =\frac13$ when rotated through one of its two ends. 
\end{description}

Note that in the last entry, it matters around which axis we are trying to spin the object. Actually, this is true for any of the shapes: All the rotational inerti\ae{} given above are specific to a natural axis. You need more information to calculate the rotational inertial around another axis. We will return to this point below when we introduce the Parallel Axis Theorem.

\begin{example}
A round object with rotational inertial $I$ rolls from rest down an incline without slipping. If the vertical distance it traveled is $h$, how fast is it going when it gets to the bottom?\footnote{If such a problem is given in terms of angle $θ$ and distance $d$ along ramp, substitute $h=d\sinθ$.} 
\begin{solution}
This question looks impossible to answer since you are only told the height and the moment of inertia. Let's just introduce whatever else we need and see how far we get with energy methods. Introducing the mass $m$ of the object, the change in gravitational potential energy in this process is $mgh$. This work results in a final kinetic energy, which on general grounds is $K=\frac12 mv² +\frac12Iω²$. Since the object is round, it should have some radius we will call $r$. Since it rolls without slipping, its speed will be $v=rω$. Finally, by dimensional analysis, there should be some number $ε$ such that $I=ε mr²$. This gives for the kinetic energy $K=\frac12 mv² +\frac12ε mr²ω²=(1+ε)\frac12 mv²$. Putting it together with the work done by gravity (Work-KE Theorem)
$$
mgh = (1+ε)\frac12 mv²
$$
Aha! The mass cancels out, as we are used to by now, but in addition the radius only appears through the combination $ε \frac I{mr²}$. Therefore
\eq{\label{E:RollingKEv}
v = √{\frac {2gh}{1+ε}}
}
\end{solution}
\end{example}
This is a remarkable result! Not only does the speed not depend on the mass, it doesn't even depend on the size! Besides the height, the only thing it depends on is how the mass is distributed within the object, not the size or the mass separately. 

An immediate corollary of this analysis is that anything that can slide down the ramp without having to roll will have the highest speed at the bottom. For example, a cart acts like a frictionless object “sliding” down the ramp. (In reality, it has wheels that roll without slipping, but these are specifically designed to have very low inertia.)
Let us also note that this relation is true for all heights $y$ between 0 and $h$, so the faster object will have the higher speed throughout the motion and therefore will always be leading throughout the motion.

Therefore, in a race to the bottom between a cart, a hoop, a disk, a ball (solid), and a sphere (hollow), in what order to they arrive? Their order is by increasing $ε$: cart ($ε = 0$), ball ($ε = \frac25=0.4$), disk ($ε = \frac12 =0.5$), sphere ($ε = \frac23≈0.7$), hoop ($ε=1$).

\end{exercise}

\subsubsection{Center of mass}
%The axes for all but the last of the rotational inertiae given above passed through a special point called the {\em center of mass}. Intuitively, this is the point with the property that for any plane passing through it, the amount of mass on one side of the plane is the same as on the other. 
%
%Consider two particles of equal mass $m$ separated by a distance $r$. The ...

The objects described above are rigid objects as consisting of constituent masses $m₁, m₂, ... , m_N$ located at positions $𝒓₁ , 𝒓₂ , ..., 𝒓_N$. Typically, $N$ is a very large number since macroscopic objects consist of particles many times Avogadro's number. The total mass of the object is obtained by adding up all the constituent masses 
$$
M = \sum_{j=1}^N m_j = m₁ + m₂ + \cdots + m_N
$$
In geometry, we sometimes talk about the geometric center of a shape. It is found by averaging the positions of the points. The language may be different, but the answer can be written as $𝒓_{center} =\frac1N \sum 𝒓_j$. For example the center of a circle is exactly where you think it is because for every point $𝒓_j$ on the circle, there is another point on the opposite side with coordinate $-𝒓_j$. Adding them all up, you get pairwise cancelation, so $𝒓_{center} = \bf 0$ (provided you center your coordinates at $\bf 0$).

In a physical object, we also want to find the center, but it can happen that the physical object is not uniform. When that is the case the constituent mass $m_j$ at a point $𝒓_j$ for some $j$ is not equal to the mass $m_i$ at some different point $𝒓_i$ with $i≠ j$. Because of this, the effect that forces have on this object will be different at different points. To find the overall point where the average forces act, need to find the {\em center of mass}. We do this not by taking the sum of the constituent coordinates $\{𝒓_j\}_{j=1}^N$ but the {\em weighted} sum 
$$
\sum_{j=1}^N m_j 𝒓_j 
$$
This gives an average vector, but it does not have the units of length, so we divide by the total mass $M = \sum_{j=1}^N$ and consider the following quantity 
$$
𝒓_{CM} = \frac1M \sum_{j=1}^N m_j 𝒓_j 
$$
Note that for a uniform object, all the $m_j$ are equal $m_j = \frac MN$ for all $j =1, ... , N$, and the center of mass reduces to the geometric center of the object. 

We can now define the velocity of the CM and the acceleration of the CM. Assuming the constituent masses are not changing in time, these are just what you would expect:
$$
𝒗_{CM} = \frac1M \sum_{j=1}^N m_j 𝒗_j 
$$
and 
$$
𝒂_{CM} = \frac1M \sum_{j=1}^N m_j 𝒂_j 
$$
Note that the sum of the momenta of a system of particles is equal to the total mass of the system times the CM velocity
$$
\sum_{j=1}^N m_j 𝒗_j = M 𝒗_{CM} =𝒑_{CM}
$$
\begin{exercise}
Prove Newton's Second Law for the center of mass of a composite object: The net force $𝑭_{net} = \sum_j 𝑭_j$ on a system of particles is given by the total mass of the system times the acceleration of the center of mass
$$
𝑭_{net} = \frac{Δ𝒑_{CM}}{Δt} = M 𝒂_{CM} 
$$
\end{exercise} 
We can draw some powerful conclusions from these results:
The importance of this statement is that, no matter how complicated the shape of a composite object, its center of mass satisfies Newton's Laws.

\paragraph{Example: Center of mass of a rod}
We will now calculate the center of mass of a rod of mass $M$ and length $L$ by chopping it into $N$ pieces of mass $m=\frac MN$ and lengths $ℓ = \frac LN$. Putting the $0$ of coordinates on one end, the center of the $j^{\textrm{th}}$ piece is at $x_j = \frac ℓ2 (2j-1)$, so the center of mass of the rod is at
$$
x_{CM} = \frac1M \sum_{j=1}^N m_j x_j 
	= \frac mM  \frac ℓ2 \sum_{j=1}^N  (2j-1)
	= \frac ℓ{2N}  \left(2\sum_{j=1}^N j-  \sum_{j=1}^N  1\right)
	= \frac ℓN \sum_{j=1}^N j- \frac ℓ2
$$
In the last step, we did the sum $\sum_{j=1}^N  1 = 1+1+\cdots 1 = N$. To do the other sum, there is a trick: First take {\em double} the sum and then rearrange it like this:
\al{
2 \sum_{j=1}^N j &= 2 (1+2 +3+\cdots +N) 
	= [1+2 +3+\cdots +N] + [N+(N-1) +(N-2)+\cdots +1]
\cr	
	&= (N+1) + (N+1) + \cdots + (N+1)  \quad \quad \textrm{($N$ times)}
\cr	
	&= N(N+1) 
}
Therefore, we have demonstrated the useful result that adding up $N$ consecutive integers gives 
$$
\sum_{j=1}^N j = \frac {N(N+1)}2
$$
Returning to our calculation of the center of mass, we find 
$$
x_{CM} 
	= \frac ℓ{N} \frac{N(N+1)}2 - \frac ℓ2 
	= \frac ℓ2  (N+1) - \frac ℓ2 
	= \frac {ℓN}{2} 
	= \frac L2
$$

Of course this is not only a silly calculation to do, it is a silly way to do it. Had we put the origin of the $x$ coordinates in the middle, the mass $m_j$ at $x_j$ would give a contribution that is canceled by a term on the other side with coordinate $-x_j$. This would tell us that $x_{CM}=0$, which is the same point as $x_{CM} = \frac L2$ in the previous system of coordinates. 
The point here is just to see how an actual calculation would go so that we might have a better understanding of related results.

\subsubsection{Parallel Axis Theorem}
As we have seen, the rotational inertia--and therefore, the moment of inertia--depends on the axis around which we rotate. 
Suppose that we choose the axis to pass through the center of mass and that the moment of inertia through this axis is $I_{CM}$. The parallel axis theorem states that the moment of inertia $I$ through a different axis {\em parallel} to the first is given by $I = I_{CM} + M d²$, where $d$ is the distance between the original axis (through the center of mass) and the new one. 
This is the 
\begin{theorem}[Parallel Axis Theorem]
Let $I_{CM}$ denote the moment of inertia of an object of mass $M$ through an axis passing through the center of mass of the object. Let $I$ denote the moment of inertia around an axis parallel to the first and separated from it by a distance $r$. Then 
\eq{\label{E:ParallelAxis}
I = I_{CM} + Mr² 
}
\end{theorem}

\begin{exercise}
%Let's test this on the rod we had above. The center of mass of the rod is at $x_{CM}=L/2$, as we found previously. We also found that the rotational inertia around an axis passing through one end was given by $I=\frac13 ML²$. This is a distance $d = L/2$ away from the center of mass. Putting this together, the Parallel Axis Theorem implies that $I_{CM} = (1/3 - 1/4) ML² =\frac1{12}ML²$.
Use \eqref{E:IrodEnd} and the Parallel Axis Theorem to show that 
\eq{\label{E:IrodMiddle}
I_{rod,middle}  = \frac1{12} ML²
}
for a rod mass $M$ and length $L$ rotated through its center.
\end{exercise}

We can confirm this by modifying our explicit calculation for $I$, but let us be lazy and make the following clever argument: The sum for $I_{CM}$ is modified because $r_⟂$ is now the distance to the center of mass $x_{CM}=L/2$ instead of the distance to one end. We can still use our sum if we recognize that we can start at $x=0$ and sum only the first $N/2$ terms to get to the middle, and finally multiply the whole thing by 2 to get both sides:\footnote{If $N$ is not even, we can either split off the first term in explicitly or sum only to $\tfrac12(N-1)$. Either way, we will get a correction that goes $→ 0$ as $N→ ∞$. }
$$
I = 2 \sum_{j=1}^{N/2} m_j x²_j
$$
In the explicit calculation we did previously, w saw that the important part of the sum scales like $\sum_{j=1}^N j² ~ \frac16 N³ + ...$, so if we replace $N→ N/2$, we get an extra factor of $1/2³ = 1/8$. However, we doubled the sum, so the relevant factor is reduced to $1/4$. Because of this, $I_{CM} → \frac14  I = \frac1{12} ML²$. 
All the terms we ignored have additional factors of $1/N$, so they all vanish when we take the number of parts $N→ ∞ $. Thus, we can see more-or-less explicitly where the extra factor of $1/4$ comes from, and we have confirmed the result \eqref{E:IrodMiddle}.

Let us end by remarking on the structure of the Parallel Axis Theorem: The equation for $I$ reduces to $I_{CM}$ when $r→ 0$, as required. Furthermore, if the object in question is a point mass or a linear mass lying along the axis of rotation, it has no intrinsic moment of inertia so $I_{CM}→ 0$ and $I→ Mr²$ which is the correct moment of inertia for a mass $M$ in rotation with radius $r$. Thus, we can think of the Parallel Axis Theorem as stating that the inertia of an object is a combination of it's “intrinsic” inertia $I_{CM}$ and the rotational inertia of its orbital motion.

\begin{example}
In class, we discussed the kinetic energy of a wheel $K = K_{trans}+K_{rot}$. Here $K_{trans}= \frac12 mv²$ is the translational part and $K_{rot} = \frac12 I ω²$ is the rotational part. If the wheel is modeled by a hoop, then $I=mr²$ where $m$ and $r$ are the mass and the radius, respectively. 
If the wheel is rolling without slipping, we argued that then $v=rω$. Therefore, the rotational inertia $K_{rot} =\frac12 mr² ω² = \frac12 mv² =K_{trans}$, so the total kinetic energy is $K=2K_{trans}$ in this case. 

We can show this a different way by using the parallel axis theorem to calculate the rotational inertia of the wheel around the stationary point touching the ground. Since that point is a distance $r$ removed from the axis of the wheel, we get that the rotational inertia is shifted to 
$$
I = I_{CM}+mr² = 2 mr². 
$$
Then the rotational kinetic energy due just to this type of “rotation” is $K=\frac12 2mr² ω² =2 \frac12 mv² = 2K_{trans}$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Angular Momentum}

\rightfig{../Rotations/ClayPlate}{55}{A lump of clay of mass $m$ is dropped on a spinning plate with rotational inertia $I$ in example \ref{Eg:ClayPlate}.}

The next concept after inertial mass to be defined in our progression through linear dynamics was linear momentum $𝒑$, Newton's concept of “quantity of motion”. When the object had a constant mass $m$, the momentum could be written in terms of it and the velocity as $𝒑 = m𝒗$. 


Continuing with our analogy, the rotational analog of speed is angular speed $ω$, and we have just concluded that the rotational analog of inertial mass is rotational inertia $I$. We, therefore, propose to call the rotational analog of linear momentum {\em angular momentum} $L$ and to define it as 
\begin{definition}[Angular momentum]
The angular momentum $L$ of an object of rotational inertia $I$ and angular velocity $ω$ is 
$$
L = I ω 
$$
\end{definition}

In class, we spun around on the heavy rotating plate with 1 kg masses in each hand. We held our arms out, initiated the spin, and then pulled our arms in. When we did that, we were reducing our rotational inertia, and we noticed that our rotational speed increased. We conjectured that in the process, the product $Iω$ stays constant, and concluded that this must be the rotational analog of conservation of linear momentum: 
\begin{quote}
\boxed{\textrm{For an isolated system, angular momentum is conserved.}}
\end{quote}
%This is the rotational version of Newton's First Law.
This is the rotational analog of the law of conservation of linear momentum for an isolated system. 

 
\begin{example} 
Suppose when your arms are pulled in you have a rotational inertia of $I₁ = 20\ kg\cdot m²$, but when they are outstretched the inertia increases to $I₂ = 25\ kg\cdot m²$. Suppose that with outstretched arms, you are rotating on the spinning platter with angular speed $ω₂ = 0.5\ Hz$. Calculate the work you do when you pull in your arms. 

\begin{solution}
By conservation of angular momentum ${I₁}ω₁ ={I₂} ω₂$ so $ω₁ =\frac{I₂}{I₁} ω₂$. By the work-kinetic energy theorem, the work you do is $W_{net}= \frac12 I₁ ω₁² - \frac12 I₂ ω₂²$. So 
$$
W_{net} = \frac12 \frac{I₂}{I₁} \left( I₂-I₁ \right) ω₂²
$$
gives $W_{net} ≈ 0.78\ J$. 
\end{solution}
\end{example}


\begin{example}\label{Eg:ClayPlate}

\subsection{Torque}

In the chapter on work and energy, we studied a problem in which the work done on a rock by a lever was equal to the work done by a force pushing on the other side of the lever. 

\rightfig{../Rotations/Torque}{35}{A lever as a force multiplier}

In this example, the work due to the force applied is $W_{in}=𝐅_{in} \cdot Δ {\bf r}_{in}$. Here the dot product takes only that part of the force along the displacement $Δ {\bf r}_{in}$, or--equivalently--that part of the displacement along the force. If the part of the lever on the left has length $ℓ_{in}$, this gives $W_{in}= F_{in} ℓ_{in}\cos θ$. 
% where $ℓ_{in}$ is the length of the lever arm on the left. Remember that although the dot product usually has a cosine in it, that is for the angle between the $𝐅_{in}$ and $Δ {\bf r}_{in}$. Switching to the angle indicated by $90^∘$. 
Repeating this calculation for the output on the right, we find $W_{out} = F_{out} ℓ_{out}\cos θ$. Setting these equal, we found that 
$$
F_{out} = \frac{ℓ_{in}}{ℓ_{out}} F_{in}
$$
and we concluded that as long as the lever on the left is longer than that on the right, we could get a larger force out than we put in. 

Notice that this type of force causes a rotation about the pivot point (also called the “fulcrum”). Also notice, that if we take ${\bf ℓ}_{in}$ to denote the vector pointing from the fulcrum to the point where the force is applied then 
$$
|| {\bf ℓ}_{in} × 𝐅_{in}|| = F_{in} ℓ_{in}\sin θ
$$
The cross product computes the part of the force {\em orthogonal} to the pivot arm, or--equivalently--the length of the part of the arm that is orthogonal to the force. The other component of the force is directed toward the fulcrum and does not help turn the lever. 

This type of turning force is called “torque”. Notice that by construction is has the same units as work, but that is where the analogy ends. Where the work is a scalar quantity, the torque is a vector. In the example just described, that vector points in the direction out of the page. Since we want to emphasize that torque is different from work, we never use the units of energy to describe it. Instead, the units of torque are Newton-meters or foot-pounds or something equivalent. The Greek letter $τ$ we will use for torque is pronounced “tau”. 

\begin{definition}[Torque]
The torque $\bf τ$ that a force $𝐅$ exerts on an object around a point is given in terms of the displacement vector $\bf r$ from that point to where the force is applied by 
$$
\bf τ = {\bf r} × {\bf F}
$$
\end{definition}
Note that this implies that the direction is given by the right-hand rule and the magnitude $τ = rF\sin θ$, where {\em $θ$ denotes the angle between $\bf r$ and $𝐅$}. 

\begin{exercise}
Show that the magnitude of the torque is the part $F_⊥$ of the force perpendicular to $\bf r$ times the length of $\bf r$ 
$$τ=F_⊥ r$$ 
Draw the triangle geometry that demonstrates this. 
Show that the magnitude of the torque is also the product of the magnitude of the force and the perpendicular distance $r_⊥$ from the axis of rotation to the line along which the force acts.
$$τ=F r_⊥$$ 
Draw this interpretation in your diagram as well. 
\end{exercise}

\begin{definition}
The perpendicular distance $r_⊥$ from the axis of rotation to the line along which the force acts is called the {\em lever arm}.
\end{definition}

For a given force, the maximal torque will result when we maximize the lever arm. In practice, we often try to adjust the force to maximize the lever arm by 
\begin{enumerate}
\item apply the force perpendicularly to $\bf r$, and
\item make the lever arm as large as possible.
\end{enumerate}
For example, when you need to open a heavy door, you will generally not push on the door anywhere near the hinge. Instead, you will push perpendicularly to the door as far away from the axis of the hinge as is practical.

\begin{example}\label{Eg:Stick-on-string}
Consider a stick of length $ℓ=1.0\ m$ and mass $M=86\ g$ attached to a string a distance $d = 30\ cm$ from one end and making an angle $θ = 60^∘$ with it as in \ref{F:../Rotations/StickOnString}. If the stick is released from rest, what is the torque around the point where the string is attached?

Gravity acts with a force $F_g = Mg≈ 0.84\ N$ on the center of mass of the stick located at a distance $\frac ℓ2≈ 50\ cm$ from the end of the end. The distance of this point from the pivot (where the string is attached) is $r= \frac ℓ2-d ≈ 0.20\ m$. 
The vector appearing in the torque is the one with this magnitude pointing from the pivot to the center of mass. The RHR tells us, then, that the direction the torque points is into the page. This is  in line with our expectation that the stick will rotate clockwise. 

The angle between $\bf r$ and $𝐅_g$ is {\em not} $θ$. Instead it is $180^∘ - θ$. However $\sin (180^∘ - θ) = -\sin ( - θ) = \sin θ$, so we still find that $τ = rF\sin (180^∘ - θ) = rF\sin θ ≈ 0.15\ N\cdot m$.
\end{solution}

In this exercise, we found that a force acting on a particle of mass $m$ at a fixed distance $r$ from a central point will accelerate the particle according to $τ = I α$.
For a rigid system consisting of an enormous number $N$ of point particles, all that would change is that the moment of inertial would be the one due to all the particles $I→ ∑_{i=1}^N I_i$: Since the object is rigid, all points must have the same angular motion, so pushing one of them results in all of them having to accelerate at the same rate. 

In general, there could be a bunch of forces acting on the merry-go-round each generating their own torque. Adding all the torques will result in a net torque ${\bf τ}_{net} =∑_i {\bf τ}_i$. It is then this net torque that will cause an angular acceleration:
$$
\boxed{τ_{net} = I α }
$$
This is the rotational version of Newton's Second Law. 

\begin{example}
Consider again the stick and string from example \ref{Eg:Stick-on-string}. Calculate the angular acceleration when the stick is released. 
\begin{solution}
This is an application of the parallel axis theorem: The torque $τ_{net}≈ 0.15\ N\cdot m$ we calculated previously acts around the point held by the string a distance $d≈ 30\ cm$ from the end, or $r=\frac L2-d ≈0.20\ m$ from the center of mass. The PAT then tells us that $I=I_{CM}+Mr² ≈ 0.011\ kg \cdot m²$ where $I_{CM} = \frac 1{12} ML² ≈ 7.2 × 10⁻³\ kg\cdot m²$ was the value of the moment of inertia through the center of the stick.  Using this value in Newton's second law for rotations, we calculate that the angular acceleration is 
$
α =\frac{τ_{net} }{I}≈ 14\ rad/s²
$.
\end{solution}
\end{example}

\begin{itemize}

Solving for $a$ is easier now because all the $a$-terms are already collected, and we again find \eqref{E:YoYoa}.

This example shows that it is possible for an object to have such a factor $ε>1$ when the radius of the circle around which the object rolls is not the largest radius. Although we cannot change $I$, some geometries allow us to roll around a circle with radius that is smaller than the overall size of the object. Precisely this is what makes the yo-yo a fun toy.

\begin{enumerate}
	\item Calculate the tension in the string on the left and the right sides. 
	\item Calculate the acceleration of the larger mass. 
	\item If the pulley has some friction with torque $τ_f$, what is the acceleration of the larger mass?
\end{enumerate}
\begin{solution}
We begin by drawing a FBD. This time the pulley is included as a disk with forces acting at specific points. 
Newton's second law becomes
$$
m_l a = m_l g - T_l 
\quad \quad,\quad \quad
I α = τ_{net} = (T_l -T_r)r 
\quad \quad,\quad \quad
m_r a = T_r - m_r g 
$$
The structure of these equations is as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item The direction of positive acceleration is chosen as downward on the left, because that is the larger mass. The tension in the string is keeping the left mass from accelerating at $9.8\ m/s²$. 
\item The acceleration of the mass on the right is upwards and the same size as the acceleration on the left because the string does not stretch. This is the opposite direction of gravity and along the right-side tension. 
\item The difference in tensions is causing a torque on the pulley. In terms of direction, the torque is out of the page, as determined by the RHR. 
\item Since the tensions are tangential, they perpendicular to the lines connecting the center to the points where the string is tangential. Thus, the moment arms are just $r$ and the torque on the left is $T_l r$ out of the page while that on the right is $T_l r$ into the page. Thus $τ_{net} = T_l r - T_l r$. This torque equals the rotational inertia of the pulley times the angular acceleration of the pulley by Newton's Second Law for rotations. 
\end{itemize}
We can solve for the acceleration by multiplying the first and third equations by $r$ and then adding them all up. 
$$
(m_l +m_r) ra + Iα = (m_l -m_r)rg
$$
Multiplying again by $r$ to convert $α → a = rα$ (rolling without slipping), we get
$$
[(m_l +m_r) r² +I ]a  = (m_l -m_r)r² g
\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad
%a  =\frac {(m_l -m_r)r²}{(m_l +m_r) r² +I }  g
a  =\frac {m_l -m_r}{m_l +m_r  + \frac I{r²} } g
$$
Note that in the limit that the pulley is ideal ($I→ 0$), we recover \eqref{E:Atwooda}. Just as in that case, the numerator is proportional to the difference in masses whereas the denominator is a corrected version of the total mass of the system: Recalling that $I=ε mr²$ we see that $\frac I{r²}$ is the mass of the pulley up to a geometric factor $ε$ describing how the mass is distributed. (We expect $ε$ to lie between $\frac12$ for a solid disk and 1 for a wheel.)

%We can clarify the meaning of this by recognizing that if the pulley were a wheel with all its mass concentrated at its edge, then $\frac I{r²}= {m_o}$ would be the mass of the pulley. Then 
%$$
%a  =\frac {(m_l -m_r)}{m_l +m_r  + m_o }  g
%$$
%and we check that in the limit that the pulley is ideal ($m_o→ 0$), we recover \eqref{E:Atwooda}. 
%In this form we see that the denominator is just the mass of the system again. 

The tensions now follow by plugging this into the first and third equations. The algebra is unpleasant, but the result can be made to look like 
$$
T_l =\left[ \frac {2{m_r}  + \frac I{r²}}{m_l + m_r  + \frac I{r²} }  \right] m_l g
\quad \quad \textrm{and} \quad \quad

\begin{example} 
If we drop a roll of toilet paper, it will accelerate toward the ground at $g=9.8\ m/s²$, but if we hold the end of the paper a fixed height above the ground and then drop the roll, it will accelerate less fast because it must unroll to fall. Suppose the roll has mass $164\ g$, an outer radius of $6.5\ cm$ and an inner radius of $2.0\ cm$. How long does it take the roll to fall a distance of $1.5\ m$? How much longer does it take than free fall?

\begin{solution}
There are multiple ways to do this problem. One way that comes to mind is to use energy conservation to get the final speed and then to use that and CAKE to find the time. Another is to use torque and CAAKE to find the time. We will do both to illustrate the methods. 

To use energy, we can use either the conservation of mechanical energy or the work-KE theorem. The work that gravity does $mgh$ will be converted into kinetic energy $K=\frac12 mv² +\frac 12 Iω² $ where the first term is the translational KE and the second is the rotational part. Without the second part, we would just have the free-fall case. 

The TP rolls without slipping as it falls. Because of that, $v=rω$. We can use this to combine the two kinetic terms $K=\frac12 mv² +\frac 12 \frac I{r²} v² = \frac m2\left( 1+ \frac I{mr²}\right) v²$. Recall that $\frac I{mr²}$ is the term we previously called $ε$. Setting this equal to the work done by gravity gives $mgh =\frac m2\left( 1+ ε \right) v²$. Solving for $v$, we again get \eqref{E:RollingKEv}! 

Should we be surprised? Well, in hindsight no, since the work done by gravity depends only on the height. What should definitely not be the same is the time that it takes to get to the floor, so let's see how that works. CAKE 3 gives us the acceleration as
$$
a = \frac {v²}{2h} = \frac g{1+ε}
$$
(This is less than $g$, unless $ε =0$, so that makes sense.) Then CAKE 2 gives 
\eq{\label{E:UnrollingDrop2}
Δt = √\frac{2h}a= √{1+ε}√{\frac{2h}{g}}
}
Note that this is the same equation as \eqref{E:UnrollingDrop}. 
This time is larger than for free fall time $√{\frac{2h}{g}}≈ 0.553\ s$ by a factor of $√{1+ε}$. 

All that remains is to compute $ε=\frac I{mr²}$. In exercise \ref{X:FatHoop}, we derived the rotational inertia for this geometry $I=\frac12 m (r₁² + r₂²)$. This gives for 
$$
ε = \frac I{mr₂²} = \frac12 \left(1+\frac{r₁²}{r₂²}\right) ≈ 0.547
$$
for a time of $Δt = √{1+ε}√{\frac{2h}{g}} ≈  0.69\ s$.

Now, let us start over and use torque methods. 
First, we draw the FBD for this geometry which consists of a disk of radius $r₂$, a weight vector vertically down from the center, and a tension upward and tangent to the disk. 

The tension will be less than the weight, so the roll will accelerate vertically downward according to Newton II. At the same time, the tension exerts a torque on the roll:
$$
ma = mg-T
\quad \quad \textrm{and}\quad \quad 
r₂ T = I α 
$$
The second equation can be solved for $T= \frac I{r₂} α$. Since the TP rolls without slipping, we can rewrite this in terms of linear acceleration: $T= \frac I{r₂² } (r₂α) = \frac I{r₂² } a = \frac I{mr₂² } ma =  ε m a$. Plugging this back into the first equation gives 
$$
ma = mg-ε ma
\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad 
a = \frac g{1+ε}
$$
and we recover our previous solution. The remainder of the steps are the same as those above, but note that we have gained a new formula for the tension: Plugging this $a$ into $T=ε ma$, we find 
$$
T= \frac{ε}{1+ε} mg
$$
The tension vanishes when there is no moment of inertia $ε =0$ and is maximal for the hoop $T_{hoop} = \frac12 mg$.
\end{solution}
\end{example}

\begin{example}
\label{Eg:SeeSaw2} 
Consider again figure \ref{F:../Rotations/See-Saw} and suppose that it shows a beam of mass $M=30\ kg$ and length $ℓ=3.0\ m$ balancing two masses $m₁ = 20\ kg$ and $m₂ = 50\ kg$. At what value of $d$ must the fulcrum be?
\begin{solution}
There are multiple ways to solve this problem. We solve it two ways first using the center of mass and second using torque.

There are three masses at the following three distances:
\begin{itemize}
	\item the rod has mass $M$ at the center of mass $\frac ℓ2$ from the right end
	\item mass $m₁$ is at distance $ℓ$ from the right end
	\item mass $m₂$ is at distance $0$ from the right end
\end{itemize}
If we put the fulcrum at the center of mass of this whole system, then all the torques will cancel. This is because gravity acts on the center of mass of the whole system and if the fulcrum is there, then this force cannot generate a torque. (There is no torque for a force through the pivot since the moment arm $r_⊥ =0$ for such forces.)

Let's take the origin to be at $m₂$ and the positive direction to be to the left. (We are choosing this because the diagram looks like that would be a good choice.)
Then the center of mass is a distance 
$$
x_{CM} = \frac1{m₁ +m₂ +M} \left[ m₁ L + M\frac L2 + m₂ 0\right]
	= \frac{m₁ +\frac M2}{m₁ +m₂ +M} L
$$
from the right end of the rod. Thus
$$
d =  \frac{20 +15}{100} L = 0.35 L= 0.35 × 3.0\ m = 1.1\ m
$$
Note that this is less than the distance to the middle of the rod, as it should be.

Let's check this explicitly with torque. The fulcrum is at a distance $d$ from the right end of the rod. Taking counter-clockwise to be the positive direction (check with the RHR that this corresponds to the direction out of the page), we have the following three torques:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The mass $m₂$ generates a negative torque of size $τ₂  = d m₂ g$. 
	\item The mass $m₁$ generates a positive torque of size $τ₁  = (L-d) m₁  g$, since it is a distance $ℓ-d$ from the fulcrum on the other side. 
	\item The mass $M$ generates a positive torque of size $τ_M  = \left(\frac ℓ2-d\right)  M  g$, since the center of mass of the rod is a distance $\frac ℓ2-d$.
\end{itemize}
There are no sine factors because the weight vectors are all perpendicular to the displacement vectors. These should all add up to $τ_{net} = 0$ since the rod is balanced (no angular acceleration).
$$
 (ℓ-d) m₁  g + \left(\frac ℓ2-d\right)  M  g- d m₂ g = 0
$$
At this point we can plug in numbers, but we push through with the symbolic derivation instead. Rearranging and dropping an overall factor of $g$ gives. 
$$
\left( m₁ + \frac M2 \right) ℓ  =  \left(m₁+  M + m₂ \right) d  
$$
so we again recover our formula above for the center of mass.
\end{solution}
\end{example}
Note that in the last example, we learned the following 
\begin{principle}
To balance an arrangement of masses, it suffices to find the center of mass and place the fulcrum there. 
\end{principle}

\begin{example}
A frictionless pulley with rotational inertia $I$ and radius $r$ is attached to the ceiling and a mass $m$ hangs from it a height $h$ above the floor, as in figure \ref{F:../Rotations/MassPulley}. If the mass is released from rest
\begin{enumerate}
\item What is the velocity of the mass just before it reaches the floor?
\item What is the time it takes to reach the floor?
\item What is the tension in the cord? 
\end{enumerate}
\begin{solution}
For the first, we can use conservation of energy, and for the second we can use CAKE, but we already know we will have to use torque for the third. We'll do both. 

The work done by gravity is $mgh$. By the work-KE theorem this is converted into the final kinetic energy $K= \frac12 mv² + \frac12 Iω² = \frac m2\left( 1 +\frac I{mr²}\right) v²$ where (as always!) we used rolling without slipping ($v=rω$). Equating and solving for $v$ gives (check this!)
$$
v = √{\frac{2gh}{1 +\frac I{mr²}}}
$$
This looks like the usual result, but we must be mindful that the hanging mass $m$ and the rotational inertia are unrelated. In particular $\frac I{mr²}$ is {\em not} some geometric factor $ε$ here!

For the second part, we can use CAKE. Those all have linear accelerations in them, so first we need to find that from CAKE3: $v² = 0 + 2a h$ gives $a =\frac{v²}{2h}$. This goes in CAKE1 (or 2) $v=0+a Δt$ or $Δt= \frac va = v\frac{2h}{v²} = \frac{2h}{v}$. This is (at least) the second time we have derived this useful formula, so let's write it again 
\eq{\label{E:timeFromSpeed}
Δt =  \frac{2h}{v}
}
Plugging in our result for the speed of the block (check!), 
$$
Δt %=  2h √ \frac{1 +\frac I{mr²}}{2gh} 
	= √ {1 +\frac I{mr²}} √ \frac{2h}{g}
$$
Again this looks familiar (as it should!) from \eqref{E:UnrollingDrop} and \eqref{E:UnrollingDrop2}, but we must keep in mind that the variables in each situation have different meanings. 

To get the tension, we do a torque analysis. Like all force analyses, this starts with a FBD. (You should draw this diagram!) For the mass, the procedure gives Newton II in the form
$$
ma = mg -T
$$
The torque the mass exerts on the pulley gives the rotational analog (check!)
$$
I α = r T
\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad
I a = r²T
$$
We can check from this that 
$$
a= \frac {1}{ 1 + \frac I{mr²} } g
$$
but we are really after the torque instead:
$$
mg = \left( 1+ \frac{mr²}I \right) T
\quad \quad ⇒ \quad \quad
T = \frac {mg}{ 1+ \frac{mr²}I } =  \frac {I}{ I + mr² } mg
$$
This has the correct units and the properties that $T→ 0$ if either $m→ 0$ or $I→ 0$. 
\end{solution}
\end{example}

\begin{example}
Consider again example \ref{Eg:SeeSaw2}, but this time, let us assume that the system in figure \ref{F:../Rotations/See-Saw} is not balanced. In fact, let us take $d=2.0\ m$. Calculate the angular acceleration that results if the system is released from its horizontal position from rest. 
\begin{solution}
We must calculate the torque around the fulcrum, which is to the left of the center of mass now (despite what it looks like in the figure). We expect the system to rotate clockwise (angular acceleration into the page), so let us refer to that direction as positive. Then the torque on the system is 
$$
τ_{net} = -m₁g (ℓ-d)+Mg\left(d - \frac ℓ2\right) + m₂g d
$$
All three terms are the weights multiplied by the moment arms. If we plug in the numbers, we find $τ_{net} = 3380 \ N\cdot m$. 
Newton's Second Law of motion for rotations states that ${\bf τ}_{net} = I \bf α$. Consulting example \ref{Eg:SeeSaw1}, we can obtain the rotational inertia as 
$
I= \frac 1{12} M ℓ² + M \left(\frac ℓ2-d\right)²+m₁ (ℓ-d)² + m₂ d²
= 250\ kg\cdot m²$. With this, we find an angular acceleration $α = \frac {τ_{net}}I = 13.5\ \frac{rad}{s²}$.
\end{solution}
\end{example}
