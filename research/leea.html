Effective Actions

A concise public-facing overview of work on supersymmetry, higher-derivative corrections, and low-energy effective actions, adapted from an NSF proposal.

Read the original proposal text

A central question in quantum gravity is how spacetime and matter emerge in a formulation that does not presuppose a fixed classical background. In string/M-theory, one practical way to attack that problem is to study the low-energy effective action: the organized tower of higher-derivative corrections to supergravity that captures long-distance quantum effects. The guiding idea of this project is that symmetry—especially supersymmetry, including partially hidden and non-linearly realized supersymmetry—may be strong enough to reconstruct large portions of that effective action directly.

The classic model for this philosophy is Born–Infeld electrodynamics. In open-string theory, Metsaev and Tseytlin showed that the effective action is of Born–Infeld type,[1] while Born and Infeld themselves had written down the underlying non-linear electrodynamics long before string theory existed.[2] That history suggests a natural question: could M-theory also have a distinguished leading low-energy effective action that can be recognized from symmetry principles before the full microscopic theory is understood?

Not every attractive property is strong enough to determine the answer uniquely. One might hope, for example, that a Born–Infeld-like generalization of gravity would soften or remove classical singularities, perhaps replacing the Schwarzschild solution by a regular black-hole geometry of Bardeen type;[3] many Born–Infeld-inspired modifications of gravity have indeed been explored.[4] Another possibility is to require duality invariance, in the spirit of Schrödinger’s reformulation of non-linear electrodynamics.[5] But by itself that criterion is not selective enough: many non-linear theories share it.

The sharper lesson comes from supersymmetry. Bagger and Galperin showed that the four-dimensional Born–Infeld theory is singled out by a particular pattern of partially broken extended supersymmetry,[6] and Cecotti and Ferrara had already written the corresponding supersymmetric Born–Infeld Lagrangian in manifestly supersymmetric language.[7] In that setting, one starts from ordinary supersymmetric Maxwell theory, adds the leading higher-derivative deformation, and then requires invariance under an additional non-manifest supersymmetry. The remarkable result is that this apparently modest input forces the full non-linear structure.

11D supergravity and the M-theory problem

The main long-range goal is to bring the same logic to eleven-dimensional supergravity and thereby to the leading low-energy effective action of M-theory. The key higher-derivative structure is expected to involve the familiar (Riemann)4 corrections. The obstacle is that no standard off-shell superspace is known that preserves the full 11D super-Poincaré symmetry while keeping the theory under enough control to build the invariant directly.

The strategy developed in this work is to use a reduced-structure superspace instead: an eleven-dimensional off-shell superspace with structure group SL(2, C) × G2, in which 4 supersymmetries are manifest and off shell, while the remaining 28 are still realized linearly but close only on shell. In this formulation, the constraints can be solved in terms of finitely many genuine eleven-dimensional superfields, and that makes it plausible to construct the (Riemann)4 deformation explicitly.

Once such an invariant is known, the auxiliary fields become part of the story. Their interactions are deformed, the supersymmetry transformations acquire curvature-dependent terms, and integrating out the auxiliary fields is expected to generate an infinite series of higher-derivative corrections. In favorable circumstances, this is precisely the mechanism by which a compact superspace construction can encode a much larger effective action. A closely related five-dimensional proof-of-principle was already constructed in earlier work,[8] while the specific 11D supergeometry needed for the present program was cited in the original proposal as ongoing work and was not accompanied there by a full bibliography entry.[WIP]

Simpler laboratories: Born–Infeld in higher dimensions

Because the full 11D auxiliary-field problem could be algebraically difficult—or even impossible to solve in closed form—it is useful to study simpler exact effective actions first. One such laboratory is ten-dimensional N=1 super-Born–Infeld theory. There is no standard off-shell superspace preserving all sixteen supercharges, so the proposal instead works in a reduced superspace R10|4, where 4 supercharges are manifest and the remaining ones are hidden.

In that language, ordinary ten-dimensional Maxwell theory and ten-dimensional super-Yang–Mills are already known, in classic work by Marcus, Sagnotti, and Siegel[10] and in a later rediscovery in phenomenological language by Arkani-Hamed, Gregoire, and Wacker.[11] The aim here is to identify the higher-derivative deformation of the abelian theory that produces the ten-dimensional super-Born–Infeld action itself. Lower-dimensional preliminary work shows how the six-dimensional case emerges as a relatively direct modification of the four-dimensional Cecotti–Ferrara construction,[7] but the genuinely higher-codimension cases introduce new representation-theoretic structures that are considerably subtler.

A second laboratory: the chiral 2-form on the M5-brane

A complementary example is the interacting self-dual 2-form described by Perry and Schwarz,[9] which is the bosonic core of the 6D N=(2,0) tensor multiplet on the M5-brane worldvolume. This theory plays for chiral 2-forms roughly the role that Born–Infeld theory plays for gauge fields: it is a non-linear completion of a constrained system whose symmetries are more transparent after one reduces the apparent structure group.

The proposal therefore studies whether a superspace version of the Perry–Schwarz model can be constructed in R6|4. Besides being interesting in its own right, such a construction would provide another testing ground for how hidden symmetries, reduced superspaces, and auxiliary-field mechanisms conspire to generate exact or nearly exact effective actions. If the relation to eleven-dimensional supergravity can be made precise, one may even hope to understand the chiral 2-form theory as a decoupling limit of the higher-dimensional gravitational system itself.[9]

Why this matters

At a practical level, this program asks how much of a quantum-gravitational effective action can be reconstructed from symmetry before one has a complete microscopic description. At a conceptual level, it tests the idea that partially manifest supersymmetry is not merely a compromise forced by technical limitations, but instead a powerful organizing principle. In the best case, the reward is substantial: a finite superspace construction could determine an infinite tower of higher-derivative terms and thereby reveal the leading structure of M-theory’s low-energy dynamics.
