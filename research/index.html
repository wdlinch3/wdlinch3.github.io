Research

Fundamental physics, mathematical structure, and the search for logically coherent descriptions of nature

My research is guided by a simple conviction: when a physical theory breaks down in a serious way, that breakdown is not merely an inconvenience. It is a clue. Singularities, inconsistencies, and unexplained structures are signs that a description of nature is incomplete, and that a deeper, more coherent framework is needed.

I am especially interested in places where our best theories become conceptually unstable: where gravity and quantum theory resist unification, where singularities indicate that observables have become unbounded, and where apparently successful formalisms remain too incomplete, too ad hoc, or too difficult to use as genuine foundations.

How I think about fundamental physics

I see mathematics as the logical language of natural philosophy. Physics earns our trust because it explains things, and the deepest explanations in physics are almost always mathematical. For that reason, I am interested not only in what a theory predicts, but in why earlier theories fail, what consistency problem forced a new framework into existence, and whether the new framework truly resolves the problem or merely postpones it.

This way of thinking leads naturally toward supersymmetry, supergravity, effective actions, string/M-theory, and modern geometry. It also makes me skeptical of approaches that rely too heavily on heuristic or magical thinking while ignoring known conceptual defects.

Main themes

Singularities and effective actions

Singularities often signal that a theory has been pushed beyond the regime in which it makes sense. One recurring theme in my work is that Born–Infeld-type effective actions may encode logically controlled ways of softening such pathologies.

Worldvolume approaches to M-theory

Rather than treating M-theory as an invitation to speculative handwaving, I am interested in worldvolume formulations that remain logically disciplined while still reproducing genuinely interesting higher-dimensional behavior.

Off-shell supersymmetry and fractional superspace

Off-shell supersymmetry is one of the most structurally revealing ideas in the subject, but it appears to run into severe limits at high numbers of supercharges. That tension motivates work on fractional superspace, where only a proper subgroup of spacetime symmetry is manifest.

Geometry, algebra, and the language of physics

Modern algebraic geometry, sheaf-theoretic thinking, and related mathematical structures provide a natural language for fields, defects, and supersymmetric spaces. I am interested in these ideas not as ornament, but as part of the underlying logic of the subject.

From broad ideas to technical details

This section of the site is organized as a gradual descent from accessible motivation to increasingly technical discussion. The goal is not to maintain several parallel versions of the same material at different levels, but to let a reader go deeper step by step.

A general reader can stop after the opening sections of a topic page. A student can continue into the conceptual and mathematical structure. A specialist can follow the thread all the way down to the technical problems, references, and original papers.

Research areas

For students and curious readers

Some pages in this section are written with students in mind. They begin with the physical question first, then explain why the mathematics appears, and only later move into technical terminology. The guiding idea throughout is that the mathematics is not decoration: it is the sharpened form of the logic that makes the physics intelligible.
