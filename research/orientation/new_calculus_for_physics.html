A New Calculus for Physics

From Newton's calculus to quantum gravity

About three centuries ago, Newton wanted to formulate the laws of nature in a mathematically precise way. To do so, he first had to develop the language he needed. That language became the calculus, and with it classical mechanics could be stated with a precision that had not been available before.

Newton's calculus, together with the parallel work of Leibniz, is extraordinarily well suited to the description of particles and forces in classical mechanics. But nature is not exhausted by classical mechanics, and later developments in physics required later developments in mathematics.

Gravitation eventually found its natural language in differential geometry. Non-relativistic quantum mechanics found its natural language in linear operator theory on Hilbert space. The quantum mechanics of light, relativistic particles, and interacting fields required yet another step: the language of relativistic quantum field theory. In that language physicists were able to formulate the Standard Model of particle physics.

The Standard Model is one of the great intellectual achievements of modern science. It describes the known elementary particles and the known non-gravitational forces with remarkable success. But it is not complete. Gravity does not appear in it as a fundamental force, and the theory is not expected to be the final description of nature at arbitrarily high energies.

Even if one enlarges the quantum-field-theoretic framework, the basic problem remains: a satisfactory theory of quantum gravity appears to require a new mathematical language. This is not a decorative claim about mathematical elegance. It is a historical pattern in physics. When the old language becomes incapable of expressing the necessary structure consistently, a deeper language has to be invented.

At its simplest, string theory may be described as the quantum mechanics of strings moving in a higher-dimensional spacetime. This is one of the clearest first descriptions of the new language now under development. But this simplest description is not yet directly a description of four-dimensional physics.

The bridge from a higher-dimensional theory to the four-dimensional world is called compactification. In broad terms, compactification is the process by which a theory formulated in more dimensions can be related to the physics we observe. It is therefore part of the effort to understand how a deeper theory of fundamental structure could reproduce the world described so successfully by lower-energy physics.

The broader goal is to develop the mathematical language needed for a complete theory of fundamental particles and forces. In that sense, the ambition is continuous with Newton's: not merely to add one more model to the shelf, but to find the form of mathematics in which the next stage of physics can be stated coherently.
